I

THE SETUP: HALF OF THIS CON­
V E R S AT I O N N E V E R H A P P E N E D

You had a fight last week. Or what almost-was-a-fight. Someone
— possibly you — said I just don’t think you respect me, and the
other person said I do respect you, and somehow that was the
worst answer they could have given. The thing got bigger. Or it
got quieter, which is sometimes worse. You went to bed thinking
the fight was about respect. You woke up suspecting it was about
something else, but you could not quite say what.
Or it was not a fight. It was your mom on the phone,
sighing in a particular way at the end of a sentence. It was your
roommate not making eye contact when you walked into the
kitchen. It was your partner answering your text seven hours later
with a thumbs-up emoji. Half the conversation never actually
happened, and you were already losing it.
Or it was the conversation in your own head at 3 AM. The one
with the boss. Or the parent. Or the version of yourself from eight
years ago who is still, somehow, on the witness stand. That one
does not end. It just goes underground for a while.
A few small clearings before we go any further.
This is not a script. If you came looking for sentences to
memorize and aim at your mother-in-law, you are about to be
disappointed. People who try to deploy NVC like a smart bomb

sound like hostages reading a statement, and the people they aim
it at can tell. The hostage knows. The captor knows. Everybody
in the kitchen knows.
It is also not a technique for being nicer. Nice is mostly a
performance — usually the one we put on right before we lose
it. Nonviolent Communication is not interested in your manners.
It is interested in whether anything true is moving between two
people, including the two people who happen to live inside your
own skull.
It is not therapy. Your therapist is your therapist. They have
insurance and a couch and they are paid to hold a particular kind
of attention for fifty minutes at a time. NVC is for the rest of your
week. The dishes. The boss email. The text from your sister at 9:47
on a Tuesday that made your chest tighten before you had even
read it.
And it is not, despite some of the marketing in some quarters,
a more dignified way to win an argument. If you are still trying to
win, you have already lost the only thing that mattered.

⁂
So what is it.
It is a way of speaking — and a way of listening, and a way
of talking to yourself — that, after enough practice, starts doing
something to the room. Something most communication does
not do. Language doesn’t just describe reality; it writes it. A sen­
tence like you are selfish renders someone permanent and opens a
courtroom. A sentence like I am hurt and I am longing for partner­

ship does something else entirely to the person who says it and
the person who hears it. That is working magic, in the oldest and
most operative sense of the word — not the woo, but language
that changes the state of the people in earshot, including the
speaker. Some of the clearest versions appear in Rosenberg’s own
pages, where the man is a great deal more political and a great
deal weirder than his reputation suggests.
At first, admittedly, it sounds stilted. I’m feeling X and I’m needing
Y and would you be willing to Z. Yes. We know. The scaffolding is
awkward. So is a beginner doing tai chi. So is a beginner doing
anything that touches the nervous system at depth — at the start
it looks like cosplay, and then one day it doesn’t, and you can’t
quite say when it changed. People who use this well do not, even­
tually, sound like they are using it. They sound like themselves,
only more clearly.

∗

A small confession before we move on, since the booklet is
going to keep tugging at this thread anyway. Most of what follows
assumes that the things you have been calling facts about your
relationships, your conversations, and your own inner life are
mostly stories. They are sometimes good stories. They are some­

times very, very old stories, told to you by someone who is no
longer in the room. Just notice the suspicion in the air.
Nothing in here is trying to convert you. You can put it down.
You can argue with it. You can do it badly. You will do it badly. So
will everyone you practice with. That is not a problem with the
practice. That is part of how the practice works.

II

FEELINGS AND NEEDS

I feel like you don’t care.
Try saying it out loud. Notice that the word feel is doing none
of the work the sentence pretends it is. What follows is not a
feeling. It is a verdict on another person, served in language gentle
enough to be mistaken for an emotional disclosure, when what it
actually is, is an accusation.
Welcome to the trap most of us live in for most of our lives.
I feel attacked. I feel ignored. I feel manipulated. I feel dismissed. I feel
like you’re not even trying. None of these are feelings. They are your
hypothesis about another person’s behavior, smuggled across the
border under a feeling’s passport. Look at the sentence. Where is
your body in it? It isn’t. The other person is the entire sentence.
A real feeling lives in the body. Tight chest. Hot face. The
cold thing low in the gut. The shoulder creeping up toward your
ear. Sad. Scared. Tired. Lit up. Restless. Tender. Furious. Numb.
These are yours. They cannot be argued with, because they are
not claims. They are weather.

Weather does not require the other person’s
agreement to exist.
The pseudo-feeling — attacked, ignored, dismissed, abandoned,
used, disrespected, manipulated, betrayed — is a thought wearing a
feeling’s coat. It carries an accusation. It invites the other person
to defend themselves rather than meet you. And here is the part
that smarts: you reach for these constructions hardest in the
moments when something real is going on inside you. The actual
feeling, usually something more vulnerable than righteous, is
right there. The pseudo-feeling is the lid you put on it so you don’t
have to set it on the table.
This is a thing humans do. The author does it. You do it.
Someone who has been practicing this for thirty years still does
it. They just notice it faster.
Think of it as a translation layer. The faux-feelings are still
running, still generating output — delivering verdicts about
someone else when what the body actually needed was a report
from itself. Run the translation before you open your mouth.

Not a list to memorize — a mirror. Find the one that fits what is in your
body right now.The translation always goes the same direction: from verdict
about them, to truth about you.

Ignored — lonely, scared, deflated – needs: mattering, inclusion, contact
Manipulated — angry, helpless, tense – needs: autonomy, clarity, safety
Betrayed — devastated, furious, disoriented – needs: trust, consistency, care
Disrespected — humiliated, small, angry – needs: dignity, belonging, to be
seen
Abandoned — terrified, bereft, hollow – needs: connection, consistency, secu­
rity
Attacked — scared, tense, raw – needs: safety, respect, protection
Rejected — crushed, ashamed, small – needs: belonging, acceptance, matter­
ing
Used — resentful, tired, bitter – needs: reciprocity, care, to be seen
Dismissed — stung, frustrated, small – needs: to be heard, respect, mattering
Controlled — furious, closed, restless – needs: autonomy, sovereignty, trust
Judged — ashamed, defensive, small – needs: acceptance, safety, to be seen
Threatened — scared, braced, tense – needs: safety, protection, sovereignty
Taken for granted — resentful, invisible, tired – needs: acknowledgment, reci­
procity, mattering
Violated — disgusted, scared, raw – needs: respect, safety, bodily sovereignty
Blamed — defensive, tight, small – needs: fairness, understanding, to be seen
Deceived — furious, sick, disoriented – needs: honesty, trust, clarity
Excluded — lonely, ashamed, cold – needs: belonging, inclusion, connection
Mocked — humiliated, hot, closed – needs: dignity, safety, respect

The reason it matters that attacked is not a feeling has nothing to
do with grammar. Saying you attacked me puts the entire drama
inside the other person’s character. They will defend. You will
press. The conversation is now about whether or not they are an
attacker, and whether or not you are a person who exaggerates.
The actual thing — that you are hurt, that something old got
touched, that you wanted closeness and got distance instead —
never makes it onto the table. The accusation is the lid.
Compare I feel like you don’t care to I am hurt, and I am longing
to feel like I matter to you.
The first is a verdict and an invitation to fight. The second is a
feeling, a need, and an invitation. The other person can meet it or
not. At least there is something in the room to meet.

∗

Underneath the feeling is the need — one piece of jargon that
earns its place here, because there isn’t a clean substitute for it.
In NVC, needs are not the things you want from a particular person on a
particular Tuesday. They are the universal stuff every human runs on — the
things you cannot stop wanting, no matter who you are or what kind of
week you’re having.

Mattering. Being seen. Rest. Belonging. Solitude. A say in your
own life. Safety. Contact. Meaning. Play. Contribution. The things
that, if you’ve gone too long without them, the rest of your life
starts behaving strangely.
The need is the why beneath the feeling. You feel hollow
because something in you is hungry for connection. You feel taut
because something in you is fighting for respect — for mattering,
more honestly. You feel that low buzz of resentment because
something in you has been quietly trading its own rest for some­
one else’s comfort and the bill has come due. The other person is,
at most, the occasion. They are not the cause and they are not the
cure. This sounds, on first read, like a way of letting people off the
hook. It is not. It is a way of locating the ache somewhere you can
actually do something about it.
CONNECTION — belonging, inclusion, intimacy, contact, to be
seen, to be heard, mattering
SAFETY — security, protection, bodily sovereignty, stability, pre­
dictability
AUTONOMY — choice, freedom, sovereignty, space, self-determi­
nation
HONESTY — clarity, trust, transparency, authenticity
MEANING — purpose, contribution, growth, understanding, to
make a difference
ACKNOWLEDGMENT — appreciation, recognition, mattering, to
be valued
WELLBEING — rest, food, movement, shelter, touch, health
PLAY — joy, humor, lightness, spontaneity
PEACE — ease, quiet, order, harmony, space to breathe

INTEGRATION — to grieve, to process, to make sense of loss

These are not preferences. They are infrastructure.

∗

How sure are you, right now, that the thing you just called
a feeling is actually a feeling? You have been wrong about this
before. You have called resentment what was actually grief that
hadn’t been allowed to be sad. You have called fine what was
actually the bottom of the lake.
Anger is its own field, and it deserves a different treatment than
the rest.
First: anger is a signal. Sometimes the most trustworthy signal
you have. The consuming kind — the kind that leaves you in
the bathtub unknotting your own muscles, the kind that has you
screaming until there is nothing left — at its core is saying one
thing with total clarity: harm is being done here, and it must be ac­
knowledged. That is not a malfunction. That is the system working
exactly as designed.
Rosenberg calls anger a thinking event — the body’s response
to a story you are telling about wrongness. Useful framing for
some kinds of anger. Not the whole picture. Sometimes the anger

is not downstream of a story. Sometimes it is the alarm going off
because the alarm is what the situation requires. The problem
with anger is not that it lies. The problem is that it needs trans­
lation before the people in the room can hear what it’s actually
saying.
This is where NVC’s relationship to anger gets interesting, and
where the standard pedagogy tends to botch it. NVC doesn’t ask
you to abandon the anger. It asks you to give the anger grammar
— to find the words that let the harm be named, the need stated,
the moment made real enough that someone else can meet it. I
am furious. Harm has been done. I need it to be acknowledged. That
sentence has the heat in it. It is not a calmer version of rage. It is
rage with enough language to land.
Anger runs in different modes, and over time the practice
helps you notice which one is in the room tonight.
Sometimes it is fear with a louder coat — fear of losing the
connection, fear that turning soft will get you killed in the house­
hold you grew up in. Sometimes it is grief that hasn’t been allowed
to weep yet. Sometimes it is humiliation, the body’s response to
being made small in public. Sometimes it is a much older anger,
on loan from someone in your family who never got to feel it,
running its programs through you decades after the fact.
And sometimes it is none of those. Sometimes it is just the
harm detector doing the job it was built to do, and the only wrong
move is to talk yourself out of it before you’ve let it speak.
There is a kind of anger that is just a tantrum — the wallpunching volatility of an adult who cannot tolerate the word no.

That anger takes up all the oxygen. It is exhausting, repetitive, and
ultimately an attempt to control the environment by terrifying it.
There is another kind. The kind that rises when your limits are
being actively dismantled, when you are being harmed, gaslit, or
trapped. This is not a thinking event. This is the part of you that
has been keeping an extremely accurate ledger of harm, and the
ledger has finally been opened.
Call it the golden bear.
The golden bear is not fear in a louder coat. It is life-force
standing up on its hind legs. Its anger can feel apocalyptic. It can
sound like a complete inventory of every time safety was stolen.
It is not kind. It is also not the same thing as a child screaming
I hate you because ice cream was denied. One is protest theater.
The other is life-force saying, with total clarity: harm is being done
here, and it must be acknowledged.
If you try to use NVC to translate your protective rage away
while you are actively being harmed, you have turned the practice
into a unilateral disarmament. That is not the practice. That is the
practice eating itself.
The goal is not to exile the bear. The goal is to build a
relationship with him — so he does not have to take the wheel
at volume eleven to be heard. When he trusts that you will listen
and translate his snarls — I need the harm to stop. I need to get
out. I need my nervous system to stop being used as a trash compactor
for other people’s unhealed parts — he can go back to hibernating
inside love.

The leash you keep on him should be made of
paper. Both of you should know it.

❦
Fear has its own field. Fear of losing the connection. Fear of
being seen and rejected. Fear of one’s own anger, especially in
households where anger had a body count. Fear of becoming the
parent. Fear that is actually the body remembering something the
mind has agreed to forget. The reason these feel different from
the inside is that they are different, and a lot of the work of NVC,
quietly, is the work of slowing down enough to notice which one
is actually in the room with you tonight.
And here is a sentence you should be allowed to say out loud,
even though most NVC pedagogy skips over it: I don’t know what
I feel.
That is also data. When the honest answer is I am numb, or
I am underwater, or there is something but I cannot find it, that is a
feeling — sometimes called freeze, sometimes called dorsal-vagal
shutdown, depending on whose lineage you read. The body has
gone quiet to keep you safe.
Before we go any further: picture the last time you were really
upset with someone. Picture it.
Not to reframe it as NVC. Not that exercise.

Now find your ten fingers and your ten toes. Make sure you can
feel each of them.
NVC does not work very well in that state. Almost nothing
does. The work is not to push for words — the work is to come
back online first. Slow breath, feet on the floor, leave the room,
eat something, anything that lets the feeling-finding apparatus
turn itself back on. Kathleen Macferran, one of the trauma-aware
NVC trainers, puts it cleanly: you cannot move the conversation
faster than the ability of everyone’s nervous system to digest, assimilate,
or integrate the conversation. You cannot drag a frozen body into a
four-step. The four-step needs the body to believe it is safe before
the words can do anything useful.
The work, for now, is small. When the sentence starts with I
feel like — pause. Something else is trying to speak. Find it in the
body before you let your mouth name it.
The body knows. The body has always known.

III

SPEAKING

There is a thing people do, once they have learned the surface
shape of NVC, where they use the shape to deliver the same
complaints they were already delivering, in a tone that suggests
they have done the reading. The grievance gets a clean coat
of paint. The structure is technically correct. The person on
the receiving end nevertheless feels, somehow, that they are in
trouble in a more articulate way than before.
This is the first trap of speaking with NVC: using the form to voice
complaints without doing the actual work the form was supposed
to make possible.
Here is the skeleton. Write it down if it helps. Burn it after.
Speaking with NVC reduces, in its clinical form, to four moves.
They have an acronym. The acronym is OFNR. People who love
acronyms tend to use them as armor, and this one is no exception.
What follows is a temporary exoskeleton for a body that hasn’t
yet learned to hold itself up without one. The goal, from the first
time you use it, is for it to come off.
OFNR: Observation — what a camera would have recorded. Feeling —
what is in your body, not what they did to you. Need — the universal hunger

underneath. Request — concrete enough to say yes or no to. If they cannot
say no, it was a demand.

Observation. What a cam­
era would have recorded.
Not what it meant, not who
it makes them.

Feeling. What is in your
body. Not what they did to
you. Not the verdict.

Need. The why beneath
the feeling. The universal
hunger — not the spe­
cific thing from this spe­
cific person on this specific
Tuesday.

Request. Concrete enough
to say yes or no to. If they
cannot say no, it was a de­
mand in a request’s coat.

That is the exoskeleton. Most of where you will actually live is in
the gap between the first time it fails and the first time it doesn’t.

∗

A short detour into observation.

Most of what we say to each other is evaluation pretending to
be observation. He never helps is an evaluation. He left the dishes
again last night is closer to an observation. She’s just like her mother
is a story. She raised her voice three times during the conversation about
money is what happened.
The grammatical fingerprints of evaluation: never, always,
every time, just like, that kind of person, you always make this about,
this is so typical of you. When you hear yourself reach for one of
these, you are no longer reporting. You are prosecuting.
There is a thread here going deeper than NVC pedagogy usu­
ally wants to go — a claim about what these sentence-forms do to
the people who say them, and to the people they are said about.
Feeling and need.
The previous section did most of the work here. Two re­
minders, since they will keep slipping if you let them.
Feelings live in the body. If the sentence has the other person in
it, you are not yet at the feeling.
The need is universal. Not the version contingent on this specific
person doing this specific thing. The need beneath I want you to
text me back faster is probably contact, mattering, or care. The other
person is the occasion. The need is what is actually hungry.

∗

Request, and the thing recovery folks already know.
A request is something the other person can say no to. If they cannot say
no, it was a demand wearing a request’s clothes.

Most people who grew up in households where directly asking
for things was dangerous have spent decades issuing demands
disguised as requests, or hints disguised as requests, or — most
painfully — needs swallowed before they ever reached the level
of a request. People-pleasing, codependence, the carefully man­
aged niceness of someone who learned early that wanting things
gets you hit, ignored, mocked, or left. I don’t really care, whatever
you want means something very different when it comes from the
partner who has shaped their entire life around accommodating
yours.
NVC, read through codependence recovery, is not asking you
to become more authentic in some abstract spiritual sense. It is
asking your nervous system to risk that asking directly might not,
this time, get you killed. That is the actual ask. It is worth being
honest about it.
What it actually looks like, when it goes at all.
Your friend has been canceling on you for three months. Every
time, a good reason. Every time, you say no worries, it’s fine. You
have been saying no worries, it’s fine for so long that you have
mostly convinced yourself it is true. The slight hollow in your
chest when she texts to reschedule is data you have been ignoring
on purpose.

What you actually feel: dropped. A little invisible. What you
actually need: to know you are a real priority, not just an intention.
You have never said this out loud. The version of you who
learned early that wanting things has a cost has been editing the
ask before it reaches your mouth for years.
Here is the practice, applied: find the thing in the body first.
Not the argument. Not the evidence of how many times this has
happened. The feeling — something between sad and scared —
and the need underneath it, which is mattering, contact, some­
thing like I want to be someone’s plan-A at least occasionally.
Then the request. Not you always cancel on me (prosecution).
Not are you sure you can make it next time? (pre-emptive surrender
dressed as a question). The actual request is concrete enough that
she can genuinely answer it: Would you be willing to put something
in the calendar for next month and actually hold it — not maybe, not
let me check, but hold it?
She can say yes. She can say she genuinely does not have
the capacity right now. Either answer is real information. Both
are better than another three months of no worries, it’s fine. Real
information lets you make real decisions. Fine keeps you stuck in
a room with no exits and a very polite atmosphere.
A request does not guarantee a yes. That is exactly why it is a
request and not a demand. You are handing another person real
choice. For most people who spent years swallowing their needs,
that is the harder risk — harder than the managed performance
of not-wanting. It is also the only one that goes anywhere real.

∗

NVC trainings frequently become a new arena for the same
patterning the practice is supposed to address. The overempathic listener who never gets a need on the table. The
participant who suppresses their own request because they don’t
want to create a demand. The workshop facilitator who confuses
self-abandonment with compassion and teaches it to a room full
of people who are already losing themselves. NVC can re-install
codependence in spiritualized form, with the original cost-ofasking logic now gilded with the moral authority of having
attended a seminar. A lot of the time it does. Naming this is not
an attack on the practice. It is part of the practice.
There is a mirror-image trap, and it is worse.
When the scaffold becomes a cage.
Here is a scene.
Someone is being chased around a backyard by another per­
son who is screaming: Why don’t YOu ever even FUCKING try to
use NVC? Nothing true is ever said in anger. Why won’t you just TRY.
The person being chased stops. And, because they have actu­
ally read the booklet, responds: Okay, I’ll try. When you chase me
around the yard, yelling and cursing at me to fucking use NVC, I feel a
mixture of bewilderment, absurd hilarity, and anger. My only request

is that your next actions or words be ones that allow me to go with
the joke.
It did not land. But they laughed inwardly. That felt sane.
Nothing true is ever said in anger is not an NVC principle. It is
the dominator tradition wearing NVC like a mask. It performs the
same structural move as the is-of-identity: I am calm, therefore I am
right; you are angry, therefore you are wrong — but now it has the
moral authority of a nonviolent communication practice behind
it.
Notice what has not been established: that the person doing
the chasing is calm. They are not calm. They are screaming.
They are in pursuit. Calm in this formula is not a description of
an emotional state. It is a badge applied to whoever invokes NVC
grammar, regardless of what their nervous system is actually
doing. You do not have to be regulated to claim the calm position.
You only have to be using the right language while the other
person is using the wrong one.
The person demanding NVC compliance while pursuing you
is doing something more violent than someone who simply yells.
They are enforcing a unilateral disarmament in the same sympa­
thetic activation they are accusing you of. Your anger is rendered
illegitimate by the grammar. Their behavior, at any volume, van­
ishes into the background as the unremarkable default.
The move has a name: tone policing. It does not require
conscious malice to operate. It works by shifting the entire con­
versation from what is being done to how you are reacting to it. The
harm becomes invisible. Your response to the harm is what is now

on trial. If you cooperate — soften your tone, reach for the fourstep, try to comply — the behavior that prompted the response
is retroactively vindicated by your compliance. Your trying is
offered as proof that you were the problem.
The codependence trap and this trap are the same trap from
different angles. The over-listener who can never get a need on
the table and the person who cannot be angry in a room where
calm is being used as a weapon are receiving the same message:
your interior is the problem, and here is the practice to make you
quiet.
NVC assumes a baseline: both bodies in the room have enough
safety, enough sovereignty, enough self remaining to speak from.
Remove that baseline and the tool stops applying. It does not
malfunction. It simply stops being the right instrument for what
is happening.

You cannot NVC a bear attack.
What Rosenberg actually wrote is that violence is the tragic
expression of an unmet need. The person doing the chasing is
unmet somewhere. Also true. The person being chased is also
unmet, also scared, also possibly in danger — and their anger
is not a communication failure. It is accurate information about
what needs to happen next.

Sometimes that information sounds like: I am leaving now. This
conversation is over.
Sometimes the most nonviolent thing in the room is the
person walking away.
This is worth naming plainly, because the practice’s marketing
tends to skip it. NVC is not asking you to stay. I need to leave this
conversation is a fully valid request. I need some space before we
talk about this is a fully valid request. I am not willing to be in this
relationship on these terms is a statement of need as legitimate as
any other in the practice.
There is a difference between NVC doesn’t apply here because
you’re being harmed and NVC applies here, and what it is telling you is
to leave. The second framing is more useful. It means the practice
was not taken from you. It means the practice is the thing pointing
at the door.
Once sovereignty is internalized — both kinds, the one that
lets you ask cleanly and the one that lets you walk — a quieter
grief arrives. You cannot guarantee connection. Your stuff sits on
your side; theirs sits on theirs. Soft-pedaling needs or swallowing
them only increases the distance. Speaking them cleanly may re­
veal the relationship was never what you imagined. NVC can keep
people together. It can also show you, with surgical kindness,
when it cannot. Other people become less threatening. They also
sometimes become more sad. This grief is not failure. It is the
weather after the courtroom closes.

The longing reframe.
One move worth marking, because it changes the temperature
in the room.
Most of us, when we finally get our feeling and need out, lead
with what is missing. I am hurt and I am needing partnership. True.
Useful. But there is a slightly different sentence that does different
work: I am hurt — and what I am longing for is more of our days
having a sense of partnership in them.
The first sentence says the cup is empty. The second sentence
says I am thirsty, and here is what I am thirsty for. Same cup.
Different conversation. The need named in the longing form lets
the other person feel the direction of the wanting, not just the
weight of the lack. It is harder to defend against. It is harder to
weaponize. It also feels more honest most of the time, because
most of the time, what you actually want is the positive version,
not the absence of the negative one.
A handful of smaller traps.
The robot problem — OFNR delivered so stiffly the form itself
produces distance — and the structure as weapon — technically
correct OFNR in a tone of cold prosecution, on Tuesday at 7:42 pm
I observed that you dressed up as observation — are essentially the
same trap. What is missing in both is curiosity, any actual interest
in the other person’s interior. Without that, you are just prosecut­
ing in a more articulate dialect. Any tool can be weaponized. This
is not unique to NVC.

And sometimes even with the form held lightly and the traps
avoided and the language as clean as you can make it, the true
thing still does not land.
NVC does not guarantee connection. It makes connection
possible. The gap between those two things is where most of your
actual life will be lived.
You said the real thing. Found the feeling, named the need,
made the request — clearly, cleanly, without a verdict in it. And
the other person could not meet you. Would not. Or heard some­
thing different entirely and answered that instead. Or went quiet
in a way that told you everything.
This is where a lot of people put the practice down. It feels like
a betrayal — you did the work, you took the risk, you were honest
in a way you had never quite managed before. And still.
What nobody’s introductory material explains is that naming
a need is an act of grief before it is an act of connection. You are
saying out loud: this is what I need, and I am not getting it. The
saying makes the gap real in a way that swallowing it never did.
Swallowing keeps the door open to a world where maybe you
could still get it, if you found the right moment, the right words.
Naming closes that door. A different one opens. But the grief is
real.

The grief of the unmet need is part of the
practice. Not a failure of it.

The willingness to feel what you actually need — even when the
need is not going to be met, especially then — is most of what the
practice is training. The feeling runs its course. The need names
itself again later, in a different conversation, with someone who
has more capacity. You will have practice at making the ask by
then.
The practice is for your whole life, not just the conversations
that work.

SELF-NVC

Most of NVC, if it is being done well, happens before you say
anything.
The eight seconds between someone saying I just don’t think
you respect me and your response. The eight minutes between the
text landing and your thumb starting to type. The eight hours
between the thing your boss said in the meeting and the email
you finally send at 11 pm. Most of the work of the practice — the
part that actually changes the room when you finally do open
your mouth — is the work that happens in those gaps, alone, with
whoever is currently rolling around inside your skull.
The first move is a pause. Not a long one. Long enough to ask:
This is a feeling. What story am I telling myself about what just
happened?
That single question, asked honestly, is most of self-NVC. The
rest of this section is footnotes on it.
The pause question: This is a feeling.What story am I telling myself about
what just happened?

One thing about the pause that is easy to miss: the inner mono­
logue gets louder and faster when the body is dysregulated, not

slower. The internal courtroom runs at full volume precisely
when you are least equipped to translate anything. Self-NVC is
only really available to a body that has decided, at least slightly,
that it is safe enough to slow down. Feet on the floor. A few
breaths. Something cold in your mouth. Walking out to the
kitchen. The translation work happens in a body that has come
back online — even partway. If the body is still spinning, the work
is to come back online first. The words can wait.

The thing the feeling is for.
Sometimes the feeling and the need are enough. The story
underneath is not always the homework.
Try this one. I am feeling insecure tonight, and I am longing for
some reassurance that you still find me attractive.
That sentence does not require a thirty-minute archaeological
dig. It does not require you to figure out whether the insecurity is
from your mother, your last relationship, the haircut, the lighting,
the third glass of wine, the article about hormones, or all six at
once. Sometimes the feeling is the feeling, the need is the need,
and the work is to say it. Save the archaeology for somewhere you
are paying someone to hold a shovel for fifty minutes. Right now,
in your kitchen, the feeling and the need are sufficient.

This is, counter-intuitively, one of the harder moves to learn.
Because the real trap, for many of us, is not failing to find the story.
The real trap is excavating the story when there was nothing in it
that actually needed digging up.
Over-excavation as avoidance.
Thoughtful people set their own elegant trap. Let me figure out
why I am feeling this is, often, the smarter and more sophisticated
version of let me not say what I am feeling. The analysis becomes
a way to stay in the head and out of the body, out of the room,
out of the request. Therapy and NVC and IFS and somatics and
astrology and your friend’s podcast all come pre-installed with
permission to keep digging instead of speaking. The dig becomes
the new performance.
The check is small. Could I just say the feeling and the need now,
here, to this person, and let the conversation happen from there? If yes,
do that. The story can still be there. It just doesn’t have to be the
entry point. The body wants to move. The story can wait.

Talking to yourself differently.
Now the harder part, because this is where the practice actu­
ally starts changing the inside of your skull.

Most people, including you, conduct an ongoing internal con­
versation that, if spoken aloud to a child, would be recognized
immediately as harm. You are such an idiot. Why did you say that.
Of course she didn’t text back. You always do this. Look at you. Pull
yourself together. This is the standard internal soundtrack for a
wide range of otherwise functional adults. Most of us cannot
remember a time when it wasn’t running.
Self-NVC, practiced over time, slowly replaces the language of
that interior monologue. Not by suppressing it. Not by fighting it.
By translating it.
You are such an idiot becomes something in me is mortified, and I
am longing for the thing not to have happened.
Why did you say that becomes I am embarrassed, and what I
needed in that moment was to feel competent, and I did not.
Pull yourself together becomes something in me is exhausted, and
I am longing for a few hours of not having to perform anything.

is different: it is not telling yourself something false to feel better.
It is telling yourself the actual truth, in precision-grade language,
in the voice you would use with someone you loved.
The brain, given that voice consistently for long enough, does
something. Language doesn’t just describe the self — it builds
it. The people who have been doing this for a couple of decades
sound different from the inside than they did when they started.

Sarah Peyton calls the part of you that learns to do this the

deliberately cultivating, in the same way one might cultivate a
meditation practice or a dance form — slowly, by repetition, with

harshness. The harshness still arrives. It just arrives into a room

somewhere young, that being harsh with yourself first was the only way to

critic is not the narrator. It is a part. It is, often, a part that learned
somewhere — usually young — that being harsh with yourself

first was the only way to head off being harshly punished by

is, on inspection, a frightened protector running a strategy that
worked once. It is doing its best. It is also wrong about what is
currently required.
You do not have to fight it. You do not have to silence it. You can
do the same translation move on it that you would do on anyone
else. Something in me is afraid. Something in me is trying to keep
me safe by getting there first. What does that part actually need. The
internal monologue softens, when it softens, because the critic
stops being the only voice in the room. Other voices — calmer,
more accurate, usually quieter at first — get to speak too.

Adjacent to IFS, not in competition with it.

The closing move.

LISTENING

A friend tells you something hard. Their dog died, or their boss
humiliated them, or the thing they have been afraid of for six
months turned out to be true.You open your mouth.
What comes out, if you are like most well-meaning humans, is
one of three things.
The explanation. Well, the reason that probably happened is… You
are about to deliver a small theory of why their pain occurred,
complete with reasonable people on both sides. The friend, in
this moment, did not need a theory. The friend needed someone
to be in the room with them.
The recentering. Oh my god, that reminds me of when my boss did
exactly the same thing to me last year, it was so awful… You meant
well. You were trying to say me too. What you said, in effect, was let
me have your microphone for a moment. You took the conversation
back to yourself. The friend, watching, has now learned that this
is not a place where their thing can stay the center of the room for
very long.
The fix. Have you tried… Maybe they have. Maybe they haven’t.
Either way, what they were asking for, in this moment, was prob­
ably not a list of things to try. They were asking to feel less alone
for two minutes. The fix-it response says: I cannot stay with this
with you. Let me hand you something to do so I can leave.

These are the three classical failure modes of well-meant
listening. They are committed by kind people, all the time,
including the author, including you. They are not malice. They
are mostly the listener’s own discomfort looking for somewhere
to put itself.

∗

What empathy actually is.
Empathy, in NVC’s working definition, is the orientation toward the other
person’s feelings and needs — the willingness to stay there without needing
to solve, redirect, fix, or take it back to yourself.

Empathy is not silence. Empathy is not agreement. Empathy is
not problem-solving. Empathy is not nodding while you wait to
talk.
Empathy, in NVC’s working definition, is the orientation
toward the other person’s feelings and needs — the willingness
to stay there, with them, without needing to solve, redirect, fix, or
take it back to yourself. It might involve speech. It might not. The
form of empathy is downstream of its orientation.
What it sounds like, in actual words, is often: something like —
are you hurt? are you scared? are you exhausted? do you need to feel

like this matters to someone right now? You are not asking because
you don’t know. You are asking because the asking is the act of
staying — the offer to follow them into the actual feeling instead
of pulling them out of it.
Sarah Peyton, the trauma-informed NVC trainer, makes the
most useful point in the field on this: being warmly and accurately
misread is more healing than being correctly understood without
warmth. Read that sentence twice. It collapses the entire NVCpurist anxiety about whether you guessed the feeling right.

The brain is built for resonance, not correctness.
Get within striking distance of what they are feeling, with
warmth, and the body responds. Get the exact right word with no
warmth and the body does not.
This means you are allowed to guess wrong. You are supposed
to guess wrong, sometimes. The friend will correct you — no, not
exhausted, more like furious — and now you are both closer to the
actual thing than you were a minute ago. Your wrong guess made
theirs findable.

Empathy as co-regulation.
If you are willing to follow the wire all the way, empathic
listening is doing something more specific than being supportive.
A body in distress is a body running its threat response — wired
hot, or gone numb, neither a state in which words come easily.
Another body, regulated and present, offers something the upset
body cannot manufacture alone: cues of safety. Slow voice. Soft
eyes. Steady breath. Unhurried timing. The presence of someone
whose nervous system is saying, without words, we are not in
danger right now.
Empathic listening, done well, is co-regulation with words.
The empathy itself is the regulating act, prior to and underneath
whatever specific phrasing comes out. Half the work is happening
in the room before anyone has chosen the right adjective.
It is also why an empathic listener whose own body is dysregu­
lated — anxious, judgmental, in a hurry, performing helpfulness
— will not produce the effect, no matter how technically correct
their sentences are. The dead-eyed therapist demo voice you can
hear in any badly-trained role-play is exactly this. Words, no body.
Form, no orientation. The cage with extra steps, again.
Asymmetric use.
You do not need the other person to be doing NVC for you to listen with
it. The practice does not require their cooperation.

One of the more useful things about NVC, and one of the most
rarely emphasized in the workshops, is that you do not need the
other person to be doing it for you to listen with it.
Your stepfather, in full sympathetic activation, telling you for
the seventh year running that your generation has ruined this
country: he is, by inspection, not using NVC. You are not required
to fix that. You can listen with NVC anyway. From the inside,
what that looks like is tracking the feeling underneath the rant —
probably some mixture of fear, helplessness, grief about a world
that no longer makes sense to him, pride that has been bruised
— and the need underneath the feeling, probably to matter, to be
seen as having lived a life that meant something, to feel like the
things he believed in still exist somewhere. You don’t have to agree
with any of it. You don’t have to say any of it out loud. You can.
You can also just be present and not bait the trap. The asymmetric
move is internal — what you are doing inside yourself while he
is talking. You are refusing to be recruited. You are keeping your
own interior alive while his is running hot. You are not escalating,
not baiting, not collapsing into the performance of agreement.
That is not passivity. That is a specific choice about what to do
with your nervous system in a room where nobody asked for your
cooperation. And it does, weirdly, change the room. Sometimes.
This is also where listening becomes a small political practice.
You can listen with NVC to people who do not deserve it by their
own behavior. You can listen with NVC to people whose world­
view you find repugnant. Whether you should, in any given case,
is a separate question. Protecting your own nervous system is a
real concern, especially across asymmetric power dynamics, and

the answer is sometimes no, not tonight, not this person, not in this
body. But the practice does not require their cooperation. Naming
this matters because the workshop pedagogy almost never does.

P H I LO S O P H Y

We are working from stories, not facts. You have been wrong about this
before. The story changes once the feeling has run its course. Different
ways of saying one thing.
The thing they add up to: NVC is not, structurally, a commu­
nication technique. It is a piece of working magic.
By magic we do not mean the woo. We mean the older, opera­
tive sense — what Alan Moore is doing when he points out that
to cast a spell is simply to spell, to manipulate words, to change people’s
consciousness, and a grimoire is just a grammar. What Robert Anton
Wilson is doing when he calls the verb to be a deity mode of speech.
Some sentences do not describe the world; they enact something
in it. They marry. They convict. They bless. They curse. I now
pronounce you is not a description of the event. It is the event.
Consider the basic move of NVC, then.
You are selfish renders Elizabeth permanently. It uses the deity
mode of speech: it places a permanent quality onto a person, in a
moment, by an authority — yours — and the only response avail­
able is contestation. Either Elizabeth accepts your verdict and
reorganizes herself around your judgment, or she defends, and
you escalate, and the verdict gets sharper with each exchange.

You didn’t text me back for two days, and I am hurt, and I am longing
for partnership does not render Elizabeth at all. It reports an event
that happened in a body, in a moment, for a reason. There is
no permanence claim. There is no verdict. The other person can
meet the longing or not, but they cannot be made permanent by
the sentence. They are not on trial. They are being met.
The same grammar holds when you are the one who has been
harmed. He is an abuser is the is-of-identity aimed in a direction
that feels righteous — it might even feel accurate — but it
works by the same mechanism. It renders a person permanent by
their worst behavior. Harm describes behavior. Behavior can be
named, accounted for, sometimes changed. The person is not the
act, even when the act has been serious and the pattern long.
Knowing that harm describes behavior rather than freezing a
person into a permanent role is precision, not minimization. One
person’s behavior can be far more harmful. One person can go
far deeper into denial, reversal, and coercion. The grammar still
matters: we name the pattern without rendering anyone ontolog­
ically evil. This leaves room for real accountability instead of
permanent verdicts.
Including, sometimes, yourself — the version of you who did
something you cannot forgive, who you have been rendering
permanent ever since.

That is the working magic. It is structurally the same move
D. David Bourland Jr. coded as E-Prime — English without to
be — and the same move Alfred Korzybski had been pointing at
since 1933 with his critique of the is of identity. Marshall Rosen­
berg appears to have arrived at it without going through general
semantics; the resemblance is striking enough that someone,
eventually, should write the comparative essay. The booklet is
doing it informally now.
The reason this matters is that the language is not just describ­
ing the speaker’s interior. It is changing it. Saying you are selfish
installs a particular state in your nervous system: prosecutorial,
certain, slightly armored, ready to fight. Saying I am hurt and I am
longing for partnership installs a different state: vulnerable, located
in the body, no longer at war. The grammar makes the speaker
into a particular kind of person at the moment of speaking. So
does the silence afterward. Speech is, in this sense, performative
all the way down. It does not narrate the self. It builds the self,
every time you open your mouth.

From the second chapter of Nonviolent Communication: A Lan­
guage of Life:

That is not a self-help sentence. That is structurally an anarchist

NVC’s deepest commitment, when read seriously, is to a kind
of sovereignty — yours, and everyone else’s. Connection over con­
trol is the slogan. The substance underneath the slogan is that
nobody, including you, has the authority to render anyone else
permanent. Stories about people are stories. The person is still
the person. They are allowed to surprise you. They are allowed,
even, to surprise themselves. The same goes for you. The story
you have been telling yourself about yourself for the past twenty
years is also a story.

This is why NVC sits next to the anarchist mediation lineages,
and why those lineages have been doing some of the most inter­
esting NVC work in the world. Dominic Barter’s Restorative Circles,
developed in the favelas of Rio, are NVC-rooted and structurally
non-state. Miki Kashtan’s consensus work runs on lines prefig­
urative organizers would recognize on sight. Roxy Manning,
Edmundo Norte, and Meenadchi have spent years pointing out
that NVC’s standard packaging often re-installs the dominator
culture it was meant to refuse — and that the practice has to
be retooled to do its actual work in bodies it wasn’t originally
designed for. None of these are fringe; they are the part of NVC
the workshop pamphlets tend to soften.
All of these approaches share the same refusal of a single,
fixed, judging self.
It is also why the IFS people and the somatic trauma people
have all, independently, found their way into NVC’s adjacent
territory. Because the underlying claim of the practice — that
the self is not a unitary thing pronouncing on the world from
a fixed throne, but a moving collection of feelings, needs, parts,
and bodies, all of which deserve precise language — is a claim
Internal Family Systems makes from one direction, that polyva­
gal-informed somatic work makes from another, that Buddhist
parts-work makes from a third. NVC translates between them
with a small, portable grammar.
That is, finally, what we mean by saying NVC is a hinge. It is
not a tradition unto itself. It is not the room. It is a door between
rooms — between IFS and codependence recovery, between so­

matic trauma work and anarchist mediation, between Korzybski’s
general semantics and the dharma traditions that have been
pointing at the same thing for two and a half thousand years.
Most of the practice’s actual value, lived inside, is in this translator
role. The people who have hardened NVC into a primary identity
have mostly missed what makes the practice worth doing. The
people who hold it lightly, and use it as a tool inside a larger life,
tend to do better with it.

A note on practice and time.
NVC at first sounds stilted. I’m feeling X and I’m needing Y. Yes.
Yes it does.
The scaffolding falls away. People who have been doing this
for a long time do not, in conversation, sound like they are
using NVC. They sound like themselves. They sound, if anything,
slightly more like themselves than most people sound, because
the to-be verbs and the moralistic judgments and the demands
disguised as requests have been quietly evicted from their sen­
tences over time. They do not require their feelings to be agreed
with. They do not require their stories to be the official record.
They do not press their version of you onto you. They are, in a
small and durable way, more sovereign in conversation than most

people are. And so are the people they are talking to, because the
form of speech offers that sovereignty as a side-effect, whether or
not the other person ever picks up the practice themselves.
What the practice does, given enough time, is not what the
marketing claims. It does not make you nicer. It makes you clearer
— clearer to yourself first, then to whoever is in the room with
you. It does not, despite some of the workshop language, make
you a calmer person. It makes you a more accurately located
person. The fury, when fury is what is in the room, is still in the
sentence. It is just no longer pretending to be a description of
somebody else.
In the way that contact improv is, for the people who have
practiced it long enough, a spiritual relationship practice — not
a religion, not a worldview, not a metaphysics, just a discipline of
paying attention to weight and contact and movement that opens,
over years, into something larger than the technique — NVC,
practiced long enough, becomes something larger than the fourstep. It is a practice of speaking and listening from the present
moment, in the body, with the other person’s interior treated as
real and legitimate, and one’s own certainty held more lightly
than your nervous system would prefer.
That is the magic. That is also the dharma, if you want to call it
that. There isn’t anything more to it than that. There also doesn’t
need to be.

C O N C LU S I O N

This is a practice, not a framework. You don’t master it. You install
it slowly, by doing it, badly, over and over, in actual conversations
with actual people — including the conversations you have with
yourself. The body learns the shape. The grammar starts to drift
in the right direction without your having to think about it. The
internal monologue softens, not by being silenced, but by being
translated into language that resembles love more than litigation.
None of this happens fast. Most of it happens at the pace of plant
growth. The thing changes you, eventually, because the language
changes you, but the eventually is real. There is no shortcut.
You will do it badly tomorrow. You will do it badly six months
from now, on a phone call you knew was going to be hard, and
then you will go home and brood about how badly you did it, and
the brooding will also be part of the practice, and that is fine.
The noticing afterward is the practice. The willingness to try
again is the practice. Most of it is just staying in the room.
There is no compliance score. There is no NVC police. The
practice does not care whether you got it right. It cares whether
you stayed in the room.

kitchen, after the heat has passed.
The thing you said — you don’t respect me — was a verdict.
It put the other person on trial for a permanent quality of their
character, and of course they defended, and of course the defense
made it worse. The sentence made the fight unavoidable before
anyone had chosen to fight.

What was actually in the room: hurt. And underneath the hurt,
something more specific — longing to feel like you mattered to
that person, in that moment, on that particular Tuesday.
The sentence that was true — I am hurt, and I am longing to
feel like I matter to you — was available. It was there. It might still
be there now. It might still be sayable, if the conversation can be
reopened. It might be the first sentence of a different kind of fight,
which is to say no fight at all.
The question is not whether you can say it perfectly under
pressure. You cannot. Nobody does, not consistently, not at first.
The question is whether the grammar is slowly becoming avail­
able — whether the hurt is getting faster at finding its words
before the verdict gets out first.
That is all the practice is. Not transformation. Not mastery. Just
a slight shift in the odds that something true gets into the room
before the courtroom opens.
A last thing.
The reason any of this matters is not, in the end, that it works.
It does work, in the operative sense, for the reasons covered in the
previous section. But that is not the reason. The reason is that it is
one of the few practices currently available to ordinary humans
that takes seriously the proposition that another person’s interior
is real, legitimate, and theirs — not a problem to be managed,
not a process to be optimized, not a market to be captured, not
a behavior to be corrected. And that your interior is the same.
That you are allowed to be inside yourself, accurately, in the body,

in the present, in the room. That you are allowed, even, to want
things.

PRACTICE

Smallest Possible Practice
Next time you notice the sentence “I feel like you…”
leave your mouth or your mind, stop. Place one hand
on your sternum. Ask: What is actually in my body right
now? Name one real feeling and one need out loud to
yourself before you speak to the other person. That
single pause is most of the work.
