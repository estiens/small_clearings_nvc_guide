# This Is Not What You Think It Is
## A Field Guide to Nonviolent Communication

---

## I. What This Is Not

You had a fight last week. Or what almost-was-a-fight. Someone — possibly you — said *I just don't think you respect me*, and the other person said *I do respect you*, and somehow that was the worst answer they could have given. The thing got bigger. Or it got quieter, which is sometimes worse. You went to bed thinking the fight was about respect. You woke up suspecting it was about something else, but you could not quite say what.

Or it was not a fight. It was your mom on the phone, sighing in a particular way at the end of a sentence. It was your roommate not making eye contact when you walked into the kitchen. It was your partner answering your text seven hours later with a thumbs-up emoji. Half the conversation never actually happened, and you were already losing it.

Or it was the conversation in your own head at 3 AM. The one with the boss. Or the parent. Or the version of yourself from eight years ago who is still, somehow, on the witness stand. That one does not end. It just goes underground for a while.

This booklet is about all of it.

A few small clearings before we go any further.

This is not a script. If you came looking for sentences to memorize and aim at your mother-in-law, you are about to be disappointed. People who try to deploy NVC like a smart bomb sound like hostages reading a statement, and the people they aim it at can tell. The hostage knows. The captor knows. Everybody in the kitchen knows.

It is also not a technique for being nicer. *Nice* is mostly a performance — usually the one we put on right before we lose it. Nonviolent Communication is not interested in your manners. It is interested in whether anything true is moving between two people, including the two people who happen to live inside your own skull.

It is not therapy. Your therapist is your therapist. They have insurance and a couch and they are paid to hold a particular kind of attention for fifty minutes at a time. NVC is for the rest of your week. The dishes. The boss email. The text from your sister at 9:47 on a Tuesday that made your chest tighten before you had even read it.

And it is not, despite some of the marketing in some quarters, a more dignified way to win an argument. If you are still trying to win, you have already lost the only thing that mattered. The booklet will return to this. Get used to it.

So what is it.

It is a way of speaking — and a way of listening, and a way of talking to yourself — that, after enough practice, starts doing something to the room. Something most communication does not do. Something that, if you squint, looks suspiciously like a working piece of magic. We do not mean *the woo*. We mean the older sense, the operative sense — language that, used carefully, changes the state of the people in earshot, including the speaker. We will get into that. There is a real argument to make for it. Some of it is even on Rosenberg's own pages, where the man is a great deal more political and a great deal weirder than his reputation suggests.

At first, admittedly, it sounds stilted. *I'm feeling X and I'm needing Y and would you be willing to Z.* Yes. We know. The scaffolding is awkward. So is a beginner doing tai chi. So is a beginner doing anything that touches the nervous system at depth — at the start it looks like cosplay, and then one day it doesn't, and you can't quite say when it changed. People who use this well do not, eventually, sound like they are using it. They sound like themselves, only more clearly.

A small confession before we move on, since the booklet is going to keep tugging at this thread anyway. Most of what follows assumes that the things you have been calling *facts* about your relationships, your conversations, and your own inner life are mostly stories. They are sometimes good stories. They are sometimes very, very old stories, told to you by someone who is no longer in the room. We will get to that. For now, just notice the suspicion in the air.

Nothing in here is trying to convert you. You can put it down. You can argue with it. You can do it badly. You *will* do it badly. So will everyone you practice with. That is not a problem with the practice. That is part of how the practice works.

Keep reading.

## II. Feelings and Needs (Or: You Are Probably Mislabeling Both)

*I feel like you don't care.*

Try saying it out loud. Notice that the word *feel* is doing none of the work the sentence pretends it is. What follows is not a feeling. It is a verdict on another person, served in language gentle enough to be mistaken for an emotional disclosure, when what it actually is, is an accusation.

Welcome to the trap most of us live in for most of our lives.

*I feel attacked. I feel ignored. I feel manipulated. I feel dismissed. I feel like you're not even trying.* None of these are feelings. They are your hypothesis about another person's behavior, smuggled across the border under a feeling's passport. Look at the sentence. Where is your body in it? It isn't. The other person is the entire sentence.

A real feeling lives in the body. Tight chest. Hot face. The cold thing low in the gut. The shoulder creeping up toward your ear. Sad. Scared. Tired. Lit up. Restless. Tender. Furious. Numb. These are yours. They cannot be argued with, because they are not claims. They are weather. Weather does not require the other person's agreement to exist.

The pseudo-feeling — *attacked, ignored, dismissed, abandoned, used, disrespected, manipulated, betrayed* — is a thought wearing a feeling's coat. It carries an accusation. It invites the other person to defend themselves rather than meet you. And here is the part that smarts: you reach for these constructions hardest in the moments when something real is going on inside you. The actual feeling, usually something more vulnerable than righteous, is right there. The pseudo-feeling is the lid you put on it so you don't have to set it on the table.

This is a thing humans do. The author does it. You do it. Someone who has been practicing this for thirty years still does it. They just notice it faster.

The reason it matters that *attacked* is not a feeling has nothing to do with grammar. Saying *you attacked me* puts the entire drama inside the other person's character. They will defend. You will press. The conversation is now about whether or not they are an attacker, and whether or not you are a person who exaggerates. The actual thing — that you are hurt, that something old got touched, that you wanted closeness and got distance instead — never makes it onto the table. The accusation is the lid.

Compare *I feel like you don't care* to *I am hurt, and I am longing to feel like I matter to you.*

The first is a verdict and an invitation to fight. The second is a feeling, a need, and an invitation. The other person can meet it or not. At least there is something in the room to meet.

Underneath the feeling is the *need* — and here a piece of jargon arrives that the booklet is going to use without apology, because there isn't a clean substitute for it.

In NVC, *needs* are not the things you want from a particular person on a particular Tuesday. They are the universal stuff every human runs on. Mattering. Being seen. Rest. Belonging. Solitude. Some say in your own life. Safety. Contact. Meaning. Play. Contribution. The things you cannot stop wanting, no matter who you are or what kind of week you're having. The things that, if you've gone too long without them, the rest of your life starts behaving strangely.

The need is the *why* beneath the feeling. You feel hollow because something in you is hungry for connection. You feel taut because something in you is fighting for respect — for *mattering*, more honestly. You feel that low buzz of resentment because something in you has been quietly trading its own rest for someone else's comfort and the bill has come due. The other person is, at most, the occasion. They are not the cause and they are not the cure. This sounds, on first read, like a way of letting people off the hook. It is not. It is a way of locating the ache somewhere you can actually do something about it.

A small interruption, since it has been waiting since the first page.

How sure are you, right now, that the thing you just called a feeling is actually a feeling? You have been wrong about this before. You have called *resentment* what was actually grief that hadn't been allowed to be sad. You have called *anger* what was actually fear with a louder coat. You have called *fine* what was actually the bottom of the lake. Rosenberg goes a step further and calls anger itself a *thinking event* — the body's response to a story you are telling about wrongness. You don't have to swallow the whole claim to notice the part that stings: there is a story under the heat, and you wrote it.

Anger is its own field. It is rarely just one thing.

Sometimes anger is fear in costume — fear of losing the connection, fear of being seen and rejected, fear that turning soft will get you killed in the household you grew up in. Sometimes it is grief that hasn't been allowed to weep yet. Sometimes it is humiliation, the body's response to being made small in public. Sometimes it is a boundary-protection instinct doing useful work, and the work is real — the body knows something the mind hasn't named yet. Sometimes it is a much older anger, on loan from someone in your family who never got to feel it, doing its work through you decades after the fact.

Fear has its own field. Fear of losing the connection. Fear of being seen and rejected. Fear of one's own anger, especially in households where anger had a body count. Fear of becoming the parent. Fear that is actually the body remembering something the mind has agreed to forget. The reason these feel different from the inside is that they *are* different, and a lot of the work of NVC, quietly, is the work of slowing down enough to notice which one is in the room with you tonight.

And here is a sentence you should be allowed to say out loud, even though most NVC pedagogy skips over it: *I don't know what I feel.*

That is also data. When the honest answer is *I am numb*, or *I am underwater*, or *there is something but I cannot find it*, that is a feeling — sometimes called freeze, sometimes called dorsal-vagal shutdown, depending on whose lineage you read. The body has gone quiet to keep you safe. NVC does not work very well in that state. Almost nothing does. The work in that state is not to push for words. The work is to come back online — slow breath, feet on the floor, leave the room, eat something, call a friend, drop into a body practice, anything that lets the feeling-finding apparatus turn itself back on. Kathleen Macferran, one of the trauma-aware NVC trainers, puts it cleanly: *you cannot move the conversation faster than the ability of everyone's nervous system to digest, assimilate, or integrate the conversation.* You cannot drag a frozen body into a four-step. The four-step is a ventral-vagal practice. It needs the body to think it's safe before the words can do anything useful.

The work, for now, is small. When the sentence starts with *I feel like* — pause. Something else is trying to speak. Find it in the body before you let your mouth name it.

The body knows. The body has always known. Most of what follows in this booklet is about teaching the mouth to keep up.

## III. Speaking

There is a thing people do, once they have learned the surface shape of NVC, where they use the shape to deliver the same complaints they were already delivering, in a tone that suggests they have done the reading. The grievance gets a clean coat of paint. The structure is technically correct. The person on the receiving end nevertheless feels, somehow, that they are in trouble in a more articulate way than before.

This is the first trap of speaking with NVC: using the form to voice complaints without doing the actual work the form was supposed to make possible.

Speaking with NVC, in its skeleton form, is four moves. They have an acronym. The acronym is *OFNR.* People who like acronyms tend to use them as armor. Try not to.

**Observation.** What actually happened. Not your interpretation of what it meant. Not your theory of who that person is. The thing a security camera would have recorded.

**Feeling.** What is going on in your body, located in your body. Not what they did to you. Not the verdict you are handing down on their character. (See: previous section. We have been through this.)

**Need.** The why beneath the feeling. The universal thing you are hungry for. Not the specific thing you want this person to do.

**Request.** What you are actually asking for. Concrete enough that the person on the other end can say yes or no.

That is the scaffold. Like all scaffolds, the goal is for it to come off. People who use NVC well do not, in practice, sound like they are using NVC. The four moves get internalized; they show up as shape, not as a script being recited at someone's mother. We will return to the question of when the scaffold is supposed to come off, because the answer is: gradually, and never quite all the way.

**A short detour into observation.**

Most of what we say to each other is evaluation pretending to be observation. *He never helps* is an evaluation. *He left the dishes again last night* is closer to an observation. *She's just like her mother* is a story. *She raised her voice three times during the conversation about money* is what happened.

The grammatical fingerprints of evaluation: *never, always, every time, just like, that kind of person, you always make this about, this is so typical of you.* When you hear yourself reach for one of these, you are no longer reporting. You are prosecuting.

There is a thread here that goes deeper than NVC pedagogy usually wants to go. Alfred Korzybski, the general-semanticist, called this the *is of identity* — the small lie that turns a momentary act into a permanent essence. *Elizabeth is a fool* treats a thing Elizabeth did once as the entirety of who Elizabeth is, forever. Korzybski's student D. David Bourland, Jr., proposed E-Prime — English written without any form of the verb *to be* — as the antidote. Robert Anton Wilson made E-Prime a working magic; he called the verb *to be* a *deity mode of speech*, the grammar that lets ordinary humans transform their opinions into pronouncements about the nature of things.

NVC, structurally, is applied E-Prime with feelings and needs vocabulary bolted on. *You are selfish* becomes *you didn't text me back for two days, and I am hurt, and I am longing for partnership.* That is not just a politer version of the first sentence. It is a different operation. The first sentence renders Elizabeth permanently. The second one reports what happened in a body, in a moment, for a reason. We will come back to this in the philosophy section, where it gets weirder.

**Feeling and need.**

The previous section did most of the work here. Two reminders, since they will keep slipping if you let them.

*Feelings live in the body.* If the sentence has the other person in it, you are not yet at the feeling.

*The need is universal.* Not the version that is contingent on this specific person doing this specific thing. The need beneath *I want you to text me back faster* is probably *contact*, *mattering*, or *care*. The other person is the occasion. The need is what is actually hungry.

**Request, and the thing recovery folks already know.**

A request is something the other person can say no to. If they cannot say no, it was a demand wearing a request's clothes.

Most people who grew up in households where directly asking for things was dangerous have spent decades issuing demands disguised as requests, or hints disguised as requests, or — most painfully — needs swallowed before they ever reached the level of a request. People-pleasing, codependence, the carefully managed niceness of someone who learned early that wanting things gets you hit, ignored, mocked, or left. *I don't really care, whatever you want* means something very different when it comes from the partner who has shaped their entire life around accommodating yours.

NVC, read through codependence recovery, is not asking you to become more authentic in some abstract spiritual sense. It is asking your nervous system to risk that asking directly might not, this time, get you killed. That is the actual ask. It is worth being honest about it.

And here is the part where the booklet pays off the punk-dharma promise made on the first page.

NVC trainings frequently become a *new* arena for the same patterning the practice is supposed to address. The over-empathic listener who never gets a need on the table. The participant who suppresses their own request because they don't want to *create a demand.* The workshop facilitator who confuses self-abandonment with compassion and teaches it to a room full of people who are already losing themselves. NVC can re-install codependence in spiritualized form, with the original cost-of-asking logic now gilded with the moral authority of having attended a seminar. A lot of the time it does. Naming this is not an attack on the practice. It is part of the practice.

**The longing reframe.**

One move worth marking, because it changes the temperature in the room.

Most of us, when we finally get our feeling and need out, lead with what is missing. *I am hurt and I am needing partnership.* True. Useful. But there is a slightly different sentence that does different work: *I am hurt — and what I am longing for is more of our days having a sense of partnership in them.*

The first sentence says *the cup is empty.* The second sentence says *I am thirsty, and here is what I am thirsty for.* Same cup. Different conversation. The need named in the longing form lets the other person feel the direction of the wanting, not just the weight of the lack. It is harder to defend against. It is harder to weaponize. It also feels more honest most of the time, because most of the time, what you actually want is the positive version, not the absence of the negative one.

**A handful of traps, named and moved past.**

*The robot problem.* OFNR delivered so stiffly that the form itself produces distance. Most beginners make this mistake. So do most YouTube demonstrations. The cure is practice, the kind that lets the body live inside the form long enough that the form starts living inside the body.

*The structure as weapon.* Technically correct OFNR delivered in a tone of cold prosecution. *On Tuesday at 7:42 pm I observed that you...* is, in form, an observation. In effect, it is an indictment. What is missing is curiosity — interest in the other person's interior at all. Without that, the structure is just an articulate cage.

*The fight over who is doing it correctly.* Two people, each armed with NVC, accusing each other of not really doing NVC. This is mostly funny if you are not in the room. If you are, leave. Get water. Try again later.

*The all-tools-can-be-weaponized note.* Yes. Of course. We will let that sentence sit there and not dwell on it.

---

**Examples.**

*Subtle failure.*

*"I feel like this is a pattern with you. I feel like every time I try to bring something up, you get defensive."*

This is two sentences with *feel* in them and zero feelings. *Pattern* is an evaluation. *Defensive* is an interpretation. Try, instead:

*"Last night when I started talking about the schedule, you raised your voice and walked into the kitchen. I am hurt, and a little scared, and what I am longing for is to be able to talk about hard things with you and stay in the same room."*

*Aggressive failure.*

*"You never fucking care about anything I do around here."*

The clean version of this would lie about the heat. The heat is the data. The work is to find what is underneath the heat without letting go of it. Try:

*"I worked on the bathroom for six hours yesterday, and you didn't say anything when you got home. I am hurt. I am tired. What I am longing for is to feel like the things I do here matter to you, because right now I feel like a contractor who happens to live here."*

The fury is still in the second sentence. It is just no longer pretending to be a description of someone else's character.

*The longing reframe in full.*

A friend says: *"He never wants to do anything fun anymore."*

What the sentence says: *the cup is empty.*

The longing version: *"What I am longing for is more nights where we end up laughing about something stupid at 11 pm, and I have not had a night like that in a long time, and I miss it."*

This is harder to say. It is also, almost always, what is actually true.

*The weaponized OFNR.*

Spouse, in even tones, after dinner: *"On three separate occasions this week, including Tuesday at 7:42 pm and Thursday at 9:14 pm, I observed that you did not respond to my text within the timeframe we had agreed to. I am feeling disrespected, and I am needing reliability. Would you be willing to commit to responding within thirty minutes going forward?"*

This is technically a four-step. It is also a deposition. *Disrespected* is a verdict, not a feeling. *Reliability* is being deployed as a leash. The request is a control measure. What is missing is any indication that the speaker is curious about the other person at all — about why the texts went unanswered, about what is actually happening for them. Without curiosity, the form is a cage with extra steps. The first move here is to put the form down and ask a question.

*The codependent's OFNR.*

A friend has been working up to this for an hour: *"I mean it's not a big deal, but, um, when you canceled on Saturday I think I felt — maybe a little — disappointed? But I totally get it, you have a lot going on, and it's fine, I just — I don't even know if I'm allowed to be feeling this, but, yeah."*

There is a feeling in there. There is a need in there. There is, somewhere, a request. None of them are anywhere near the surface. The work here is not refining the four-step. The work is making it safe enough — for them, and inside them — to take up the space the request was going to need.

---

The four-step is a tool. The point was never the four-step. The point is the person you are reaching toward through it, including, sometimes, yourself.

## IV. Speaking to Yourself

Most of NVC, if it is being done well, happens before you say anything.

The eight seconds between someone saying *I just don't think you respect me* and your response. The eight minutes between the text landing and your thumb starting to type. The eight hours between the thing your boss said in the meeting and the email you finally send at 11 pm. Most of the work of the practice — the part that actually changes the room when you finally do open your mouth — is the work that happens in those gaps, alone, with whoever is currently rolling around inside your skull.

The first move is a pause. Not a long one. Long enough to ask:

*This is a feeling. What story am I telling myself about what just happened?*

That single question, asked honestly, is most of self-NVC. The rest of this section is footnotes on it.

**The thing the feeling is for.**

Sometimes the feeling and the need are enough. The story underneath is not always the homework.

Try this one. *I am feeling insecure tonight, and I am longing for some reassurance that you still find me attractive.*

That sentence does not require a thirty-minute archaeological dig. It does not require you to figure out whether the insecurity is from your mother, your last relationship, the haircut, the lighting, the third glass of wine, the article about hormones, or all six at once. Sometimes the feeling is the feeling, the need is the need, and the work is to say it. Save the archaeology for somewhere you are paying someone to hold a shovel for fifty minutes. Right now, in your kitchen, the feeling and the need are sufficient.

This is, counter-intuitively, one of the harder moves to learn. Because the *real* trap, for many of us, is not failing to find the story. The real trap is excavating the story when there was nothing in it that actually needed digging up.

**Over-excavation as avoidance.**

Thoughtful people set their own elegant trap. *Let me figure out why I am feeling this* is, often, the smarter and more sophisticated version of *let me not say what I am feeling.* The analysis becomes a way to stay in the head and out of the body, out of the room, out of the request. Therapy and NVC and IFS and somatics and astrology and your friend's podcast all come pre-installed with permission to keep digging instead of speaking. The dig becomes the new performance.

This is also a thing humans do. The author does it. You probably do it. The fifteen-year practitioner does it slightly faster on the catch.

The check is small. *Could I just say the feeling and the need now, here, to this person, and let the conversation happen from there?* If yes, do that. The story can still be there. It just doesn't have to be the entry point. The body wants to move. The story can wait.

**Talking to yourself differently.**

Now the harder part, because this is where the practice actually starts changing the inside of your skull.

Most people, including you, conduct an ongoing internal conversation that, if it were spoken aloud to a child, would constitute a form of abuse. *You are such an idiot. Why did you say that. Of course she didn't text back. You always do this. Look at you. Pull yourself together.* This is the standard internal soundtrack for a wide range of otherwise functional adults. Most of us cannot remember a time when it wasn't running.

Self-NVC, practiced over time, slowly replaces the language of that interior monologue. Not by suppressing it. Not by fighting it. By translating it.

*You are such an idiot* becomes *something in me is mortified, and I am longing for the thing not to have happened.*

*Why did you say that* becomes *I am embarrassed, and what I needed in that moment was to feel competent, and I did not.*

*Pull yourself together* becomes *something in me is exhausted, and I am longing for a few hours of not having to perform anything.*

This sounds, on first read, like positive thinking, or affirmations, or any number of things this booklet is otherwise allergic to. It is not. The mechanism is different. It is not telling yourself something false to feel better. It is telling yourself the actual truth, in precision-grade language, in the voice you would use with someone you loved.

The brain, given that voice consistently for long enough, does something. We are at the edge of an argument the booklet will make in its own time about NVC being, structurally, a working piece of magic. For now, just notice that the people who have been doing this for a couple of decades sound different from the inside than they did when they started. Sarah Peyton, an NVC trainer who has spent her career on the neurolinguistic side of this, calls the part of you that learns to do this the *Resonating Self-Witness.* It is not a metaphor. It is a part you are deliberately cultivating, in the same way one might cultivate a meditation practice or a dance form. After enough years of practice, the Resonating Self-Witness picks up the shift in the body before the inner critic has finished its sentence.

**Adjacent to IFS, not in competition with it.**

If this is starting to sound like something Internal Family Systems people would recognize, that is correct. NVC and IFS are doing related work from different angles, and saying so out loud is one of the things this booklet is here to do.

IFS unblends. NVC translates. IFS works by helping the part that has been driving the bus step back; NVC works by giving the part a precise grammar to speak in. The same internal moment can be addressed by either, and they tend to amplify each other. You do not have to commit to either lineage to use the move. They are not in competition for you. The booklet is going to keep doing this — naming the practices NVC sits next to without trying to swallow them — because the small portable grammar of feelings and needs is most useful as a hinge between the bigger traditions, not as a religion.

**A small interruption.**

How sure are you, right now, that the story you are telling yourself about this morning is the actual story? You have been wrong about this before. You have rehearsed entire indictments against people who turned out to have been carrying something you didn't know about. You have spent days in a private courtroom where you were both the prosecutor and the defense, and the verdict changed once you had eaten, or slept, or talked to anyone other than the version of the person inside your head. The story is rarely the final word. Feelings run their course, and after they run their course, the interpretation shifts. This is not a failure of feeling. This is what feelings actually are. They are passing weather, and the meaning you make of them is the cloud-shape you saw at one particular angle of light.

Often you do not need to know why. The feeling will pass. The need, named, gets met or it doesn't. You move on. The story you would have written about it, in advance, would have been wrong anyway.

**The closing move.**

Most often, when the pause has done its work and the feeling has been found, what is left is the need. *Connection. Rest. Some say in your own life. Mattering. To be seen. Solitude. Care.*

Speak from there. Or don't speak yet, because the speaking will come later, and right now what is needed is the breath, the walk, the bowl of soup, the second hour of sleep you had been refusing yourself.

The practice is mostly internal. The mouth is downstream.

## V. Listening

A friend tells you something hard. Their dog died, or their boss humiliated them, or the thing they have been afraid of for six months turned out to be true. You open your mouth.

What comes out, if you are like most well-meaning humans, is one of three things.

*The explanation.* *Well, the reason that probably happened is...* You are about to deliver a small theory of why their pain occurred, complete with reasonable people on both sides. The friend, in this moment, did not need a theory. The friend needed someone to be in the room with them.

*The recentering.* *Oh my god, that reminds me of when my boss did exactly the same thing to me last year, it was so awful...* You meant well. You were trying to say *me too.* What you said, in effect, was *let me have your microphone for a moment.* You took the conversation back to yourself. The friend, watching, has now learned that this is not a place where their thing can stay the center of the room for very long.

*The fix.* *Have you tried...* Maybe they have. Maybe they haven't. Either way, what they were asking for, in this moment, was probably not a list of things to try. They were asking to feel less alone for two minutes. The fix-it response says: *I cannot stay with this with you. Let me hand you something to do so I can leave.*

These are the three classical failure modes of well-meant listening. They are committed by kind people, all the time, including the author, including you. They are not malice. They are mostly the listener's own discomfort looking for somewhere to put itself.

**What empathy actually is.**

Empathy is not silence. Empathy is not agreement. Empathy is not problem-solving. Empathy is not nodding while you wait to talk.

Empathy, in NVC's working definition, is the orientation toward the other person's feelings and needs — the willingness to stay there, with them, without needing to solve, redirect, fix, or take it back to yourself. It might involve speech. It might not. The form of empathy is downstream of its orientation.

What it sounds like, in actual words, is often: *something like — are you hurt? are you scared? are you exhausted? do you need to feel like this matters to someone right now?* You are not asking because you don't know. You are asking because the asking is the act of staying — the offer to follow them into the actual feeling instead of pulling them out of it.

Sarah Peyton, the trauma-informed NVC trainer mentioned a couple of sections back, makes the most useful point in the field on this: *being warmly and accurately misread is more healing than being correctly understood without warmth.* Read that sentence twice. It collapses the entire NVC-purist anxiety about whether you guessed the feeling right. The brain is built for resonance, not correctness. Get within striking distance of what they are feeling, with warmth, and the body responds. Get the exact right word with no warmth and the body does not.

This means you are allowed to guess wrong. You are *supposed* to guess wrong, sometimes. The friend will correct you — *no, not exhausted, more like furious* — and now you are both closer to the actual thing than you were a minute ago. Your wrong guess made theirs findable.

**Empathy as co-regulation.**

If you are willing to follow the wire all the way, empathic listening is doing something more specific than *being supportive.* The polyvagal people — Stephen Porges and Deb Dana most of all — have a framework for what is actually happening in the body of someone in distress, and what a regulated nervous system in the room with them is offering.

The short version: a body in distress is a body in sympathetic activation (fight or flight) or dorsal-vagal shutdown (freeze, numbness, the underwater feeling). Neither of these is a state in which words come easily. Another body, regulated and present, offers the upset body something the upset body cannot manufacture from the inside: cues of safety. Slow voice. Soft eyes. Steady breath. Unhurried timing. The presence of someone whose body is saying, without saying, *we are not in danger right now.*

Empathic listening, done well, is co-regulation with words. The empathy itself is the regulating act, prior to and underneath whatever specific phrasing comes out. Half the work is happening in the room before anyone has chosen the right adjective.

It is also why an empathic listener whose own body is dysregulated — anxious, judgmental, in a hurry, performing helpfulness — will not produce the effect, no matter how technically correct their sentences are. The dead-eyed therapist demo voice you can hear in any badly-trained role-play is exactly this. Words, no body. Form, no orientation. The cage with extra steps, again.

**Asymmetric use.**

One of the more useful things about NVC, and one of the most rarely emphasized in the workshops, is that you do not need the other person to be doing it for you to listen with it.

Your stepfather, in full sympathetic activation, telling you for the seventh year running that your generation has ruined this country: he is, by inspection, not using NVC. You are not required to fix that. You can listen with NVC anyway. From the inside, what that looks like is tracking the feeling underneath the rant — probably some mixture of fear, helplessness, grief about a world that no longer makes sense to him, pride that has been bruised — and the need underneath the feeling, probably to matter, to be seen as having lived a life that meant something, to feel like the things he believed in still exist somewhere. You don't have to agree with any of it. You don't have to say any of it out loud. You can. You can also just be present and not bait the trap. The asymmetric move is internal — what you are doing inside yourself while he is talking — and it does, weirdly, change the room. Sometimes.

This is also where listening becomes a small political practice. You can listen with NVC to people who do not deserve it by their own behavior. You can listen with NVC to people whose worldview you find repugnant. Whether you *should*, in any given case, is a separate question. Protecting your own nervous system is a real concern, especially across asymmetric power dynamics, and the answer is sometimes *no, not tonight, not this person, not in this body.* But the practice does not require their cooperation. Naming this matters because the workshop pedagogy almost never does.

**The numb friend.**

A note for the moments when your friend's honest answer is *I don't know what I feel.*

Most NVC pedagogy assumes the speaker can locate a feeling. Trauma-informed NVC has decent material for someone in sympathetic activation; it has less for someone in dorsal-vagal shutdown — the friend who has gone numb, or underwater, or who is functionally underneath the conversation.

The work, when this is what is in the room, is not to push for words. It is to be present without demand. Not to require them to produce a feeling you can then empathically reflect. Sometimes that means sitting with them. Sometimes it means making them tea. Sometimes it means going for a walk and not talking. Resmaa Menakem and the somatic trauma writers will tell you, in stronger language than this booklet has space for, that words applied to a body that has not yet decided it is safe can re-traumatize. The empathic move, in that state, is body-first. Words later. Sometimes much later.

This is also where the booklet has to acknowledge a thing. NVC's standard pedagogy was developed in mostly-white, mostly-middle-class settings, and the bodies it assumed in the room were bodies that had not been racialized into chronic survival mode. A feelings-and-needs script applied without somatic and historical literacy can land differently on different bodies. Roxy Manning, Edmundo Norte, and the decolonizing-NVC writers are the ones to read on this. The booklet is naming it and pointing outward.

---

**Examples.**

*The explanation trap.*

Friend: *"My boss humiliated me in front of the whole team this morning. He did it again."*

What we usually do: *"I think he probably feels insecure about the new VP. He's been weird ever since the reorg."*

What empathy sounds like: *"Oh god. Are you furious? Are you hurt? Both?"* Or just: *"That sounds awful. I'm so sorry. What was it like in there?"*

The first response delivers a theory. The second one stays.

*The recentering trap.*

Friend: *"I don't know what to do. I think my marriage is over."*

What we usually do: *"God, I went through that exact thing in 2017, it was the worst year of my life, eventually I—"*

What empathy sounds like: *"I am here. Tell me what's happening for you. Are you scared? Are you grieving already?"*

The first response moves the camera. The second one keeps it on them.

*The fix trap.*

Friend, crying: *"I just got the diagnosis."*

What we usually do: *"Have you looked into the specialist at—"* or *"There's a great support group I can—"* or *"Have you tried meditation?"*

What empathy sounds like: *"Oh, friend. Come here. What do you need right now?"*

The first response hands them a task. The second one offers presence.

*Asymmetric use.*

Stepfather, mid-rant: *"Your generation thinks everything is offensive. Nobody can take a joke anymore. The country is going to hell."*

What we want to do: argue.

What asymmetric NVC sounds like, internally: *He is scared. He is grieving a world he thought he was still living in. He is needing to feel like the life he built still has meaning to somebody.*

What it sounds like externally, if anything: *"Sounds like a lot has changed in ways that don't feel right to you."*

You do not have to agree with him. You can decline to engage further. You also did not bait the trap. The room is, very slightly, less on fire than it was.

---

The work of listening is mostly the work of staying. The words are downstream, again. Most of what you have to do is not leave.

## VI. Why Any of This Matters

You have probably noticed by now that this booklet has been making the same argument over and over without quite stating it.

It has been hiding in the asides. *We are working from stories, not facts. You have been wrong about this before. The story changes once the feeling has run its course. The mouth is downstream.* Five different ways of saying one thing.

The thing, said directly: NVC is not, structurally, a communication technique. It is a piece of working magic. We can say this now because the booklet has spent several thousand words showing it to you in slow motion.

By *magic* we do not mean the woo. We mean the older, operative sense — what Alan Moore is doing when he points out that *to cast a spell is simply to spell, to manipulate words, to change people's consciousness*, and *a grimoire is just a grammar.* What Robert Anton Wilson is doing when he calls the verb *to be* a *deity mode of speech.* What J.L. Austin is doing when he points out that some sentences do not describe the world; they *enact* something in it. They marry. They convict. They bless. They curse. *I now pronounce you...* is not a description of the event. It is the event.

Consider the basic move of NVC, then.

*You are selfish* renders Elizabeth permanently. It uses the deity mode of speech: it places a permanent quality onto a person, in a moment, by an authority — yours — and the only response available is contestation. Either Elizabeth accepts your verdict and reorganizes herself around your judgment, or she defends, and you escalate, and the small private courtroom you opened up in your kitchen runs another session.

*You didn't text me back for two days, and I am hurt, and I am longing for partnership* does not render Elizabeth at all. It reports an event that happened in a body, in a moment, for a reason. There is no permanence claim. There is no verdict. The other person can meet the longing or not, but they cannot be made permanent by the sentence. They are not on trial. They are being met.

That is the working magic. It is structurally the same move D. David Bourland Jr. coded as E-Prime — English without *to be* — and the same move Alfred Korzybski had been pointing at since 1933 with his critique of the *is of identity.* Marshall Rosenberg appears to have arrived at it without going through general semantics; the resemblance is striking enough that someone, eventually, should write the comparative essay. The booklet is doing it informally now.

The reason this matters is that the language is not just describing the speaker's interior. It is *changing* it. Saying *you are selfish* installs a particular state in your nervous system: prosecutorial, certain, slightly armored, ready to fight. Saying *I am hurt and I am longing for partnership* installs a different state: vulnerable, located in the body, no longer at war. The grammar makes the speaker into a particular kind of person at the moment of speaking. So does the silence afterward. Speech is, in this sense, performative all the way down. It does not narrate the self. It builds the self, every time you open your mouth.

If that sounds like a strong claim, it is. It is also the claim Marshall Rosenberg himself was making, and he was making it more sharply than his reputation would suggest.

From the second chapter of *Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life:*

> *Life-alienating communication both stems from and supports hierarchical or domination societies, where large populations are controlled by a small number of individuals to those individuals' own benefit... The language of wrongness, should, and have to is perfectly suited for this purpose: the more people are trained to think in terms of moralistic judgments that imply wrongness and badness, the more they are being trained to look outside themselves — to outside authorities — for the definition of what constitutes right, wrong, good, and bad. When we are in contact with our feelings and needs, we humans no longer make good slaves and underlings.*

That is not a self-help sentence. That is structurally an anarchist sentence. It identifies the language of moral judgment as the linguistic substrate of obedience. It identifies the practice of locating one's own feelings and needs as a refusal of that obedience.

The booklet is not going to argue this at length, but it is going to name it, because the field tends to pretend Rosenberg didn't write it.

NVC's deepest commitment, when read seriously, is to a kind of sovereignty — yours, and everyone else's. *Connection over control* is the slogan. The substance underneath the slogan is that nobody, including you, has the authority to render anyone else permanent. Stories about people are stories. The person is still the person. They are allowed to surprise you. They are allowed, even, to surprise themselves. The same goes for you. The story you have been telling yourself about yourself for the past twenty years is also a story.

This is why NVC sits comfortably next to the anarchist mediation lineages, and why the anarchist mediation lineages have, quietly, been doing some of the most interesting NVC work in the world. Dominic Barter's *Restorative Circles*, developed in the favelas of Rio de Janeiro and adopted by the Brazilian Ministry of Justice in 2005, are NVC-rooted and structurally non-state — they relocate accountability to the local community, using the language of *author of an act, recipient,* and *community* in place of *offender* and *victim.* Miki Kashtan's Nonviolent Global Liberation work runs on consensus lines that prefigurative-anarchist organizers would recognize on sight. Roxy Manning, Edmundo Norte, and Meenadchi — whose small zine *Decolonizing Non-Violent Communication* (2019) is required reading if you want to take this thread further — have done the work of pointing out that NVC's standard packaging often re-installs the dominator culture it was supposed to refuse, and that the practice has to be retooled to do its actual work in bodies the practice was not originally designed for. CrimethInc.'s *Accounting for Ourselves* names NVC explicitly as one of the skill-sets community accountability work draws on. None of these are fringe — they are the part of NVC the workshop pamphlets tend to soften.

It is also why the IFS people and the somatic trauma people have all, independently, found their way into NVC's adjacent territory. Because the underlying claim of the practice — that the self is not a unitary thing pronouncing on the world from a fixed throne, but a moving collection of feelings, needs, parts, and bodies, all of which deserve precise language — is a claim Internal Family Systems makes from one direction, that polyvagal-informed somatic work makes from another, that Buddhist parts-work makes from a third. NVC translates between them with a small, portable grammar.

That is, finally, what we mean by saying NVC is a hinge. It is not a tradition unto itself. It is not the room. It is a door between rooms — between IFS and codependence recovery, between somatic trauma work and anarchist mediation, between Korzybski's general semantics and the dharma traditions that have been pointing at the same thing for two and a half thousand years. Most of the practice's actual value, lived inside, is in this translator role. The booklet's view, for what it is worth, is that the people who have hardened NVC into a primary identity have mostly missed what makes the practice worth doing. The people who hold it lightly, and use it as a tool inside a larger life, tend to do better with it.

**A note on practice and time.**

The booklet has been telling you, in different ways, that NVC at first sounds stilted. *I'm feeling X and I'm needing Y.* Yes. Yes it does. We have established this.

The thing the booklet has not quite said yet, and should say before the conclusion, is what happens after you have been doing this for years.

The scaffolding falls away. People who have been doing this for a long time do not, in conversation, sound like they are using NVC. They sound like themselves. They sound, if anything, slightly more like themselves than most people sound, because the *to-be* verbs and the moralistic judgments and the demands disguised as requests have been quietly evicted from their sentences over time. They do not require their feelings to be agreed with. They do not require their stories to be the official record. They do not press their version of you onto you. They are, in a small and durable way, more sovereign in conversation than most people are. And so are the people they are talking to, because the form of speech offers that sovereignty as a side-effect, whether or not the other person ever picks up the practice themselves.

What the practice does, given enough time, is not what the marketing claims. It does not make you nicer. It makes you *clearer* — clearer to yourself first, then to whoever is in the room with you. It does not, despite some of the workshop language, make you a calmer person. It makes you a more accurately located person. The fury, when fury is what is in the room, is still in the sentence. It is just no longer pretending to be a description of somebody else.

In the way that contact improv is, for the people who have practiced it long enough, a spiritual relationship practice — not a religion, not a worldview, not a metaphysics, just a discipline of paying attention to weight and contact and movement that opens, over years, into something larger than the technique — NVC, practiced long enough, becomes something larger than the four-step. It is a practice of speaking and listening from the present moment, in the body, with the other person's interior treated as real and legitimate, and one's own certainty held more lightly than your nervous system would prefer.

That is the magic. That is also the dharma, if you want to call it that. There isn't anything more to it than that. There also doesn't need to be.

## VII. A Word Before You Go

A few things, and then we are done for the night.

This is a practice, not a framework. You don't master it. You install it slowly, by doing it, badly, over and over, in actual conversations with actual people — including the conversations you have with yourself. The body learns the shape. The grammar starts to drift in the right direction without your having to think about it. The internal monologue softens, not by being silenced, but by being translated into language that resembles love more than litigation. None of this happens fast. Most of it happens at the pace of plant growth. The thing changes you, eventually, because the language changes you, but the *eventually* is real. There is no shortcut.

You will do it badly tomorrow. You will do it badly next Thursday. You will do it badly six months from now, on a phone call you knew was going to be hard, and then you will go home and brood about how badly you did it, and the brooding will also be a form of doing it badly, and that is fine. The doing-it-badly is the practice. The noticing afterward is the practice. The willingness to try again is the practice. There is no compliance score. There is no NVC police. The practice does not care whether you got it right. It cares whether you stayed in the room.

A warning, since the booklet would not be honest without it.

For some of us — particularly those of us who already over-empathize, who already swallow our needs, who already make ourselves small to avoid creating a demand — NVC can re-install the same patterning in a more spiritualized costume. The over-listener who never gets a need on the table. The seminar-trained adult who confuses self-abandonment with compassion and starts teaching the confusion to others. The person who learned, somewhere along the way, that wanting things gets you punished, and now has been given a beautiful new vocabulary in which to keep not-wanting things. If you recognize yourself anywhere in that paragraph: the work, for you, is probably less in the listening and more in the asking. Give yourself permission to make demands the practice would technically prefer you frame as requests. Sometimes the request can wait. The asking cannot.

The point of all of this — and this is the piece the booklet has been saying since the first page in different ways — is connection, not compliance. Not getting your sentences right. Not winning. Not converting your partner. Not having a nicer marriage by Friday. Connection, in the actual sense — the slightly disorienting moment where you realize the person across from you is a whole entire human you do not get to render permanent, and they realize the same thing about you, and you both stay anyway.

That is the dharma, if you want it.

If you want more than the booklet, there is more.

Marshall Rosenberg's *Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life* is the foundational book, and as you have probably gathered by now, it is a great deal weirder and more political than its reputation suggests. Read it. Sarah Peyton's *Your Resonant Self* is the trauma-and-neuroscience-informed bridge — the book to read if the somatic side of any of this called you. Miki Kashtan's *The Highest Common Denominator* and *Reweaving Our Human Fabric* take the political claims seriously and run with them. Roxy Manning's *The Antiracist Heart* and Meenadchi's *Decolonizing Non-Violent Communication* are required if you want to take the work into bodies and contexts the standard pedagogy was not built for. Oren Jay Sofer's *Say What You Mean* threads NVC and Buddhist mindfulness practice. Kathleen Macferran on trauma-informed NVC. Dominic Barter on Restorative Circles, if the anarchist-mediation thread caught you.

Adjacent: Robert Anton Wilson's *Quantum Psychology* on E-Prime. Korzybski's *Science and Sanity* if you have the patience. Alan Moore on language as magic in *On Magic* and the *Bumper Book of Magic.* Resmaa Menakem's *My Grandmother's Hands* on racialized somatic trauma. Bessel van der Kolk's *The Body Keeps the Score* on the body that holds what the mind has agreed to forget. Deb Dana on polyvagal theory in plain language.

Practice groups exist. Most of them do not advertise. They meet in living rooms and on video calls and in slightly awkward circles in community centers, and they are, like all groups, mixed bags of useful and unhelpful, depending on who is in them. Find one if it is your move. Don't, if it isn't.

This booklet is a door. The room is much larger.

A last thing.

The reason any of this matters is not, in the end, that it works. It does work, in the operative sense, for the reasons covered in the previous section. But that is not the reason. The reason is that it is one of the few practices currently available to ordinary humans that takes seriously the proposition that another person's interior is real, legitimate, and theirs — not a problem to be managed, not a process to be optimized, not a market to be captured, not a behavior to be corrected. And that your interior is the same. That you are allowed to be inside yourself, accurately, in the body, in the present, in the room. That you are allowed, even, to want things.

For some of us, that is not nothing.

For some of us, that is nearly everything.

Try it on something small this week. See what moves.
