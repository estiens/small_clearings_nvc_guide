Most of this practice happens in the eight seconds before you say anything, and most of those eight seconds are spent alone.

Something tightens in the chest. Something else — older, scratchier — starts narrating. The narrator is fast and very confident and almost always two steps ahead of the body. The pause is just this: *that is a feeling. What story am I telling myself about it?* You don't have to answer cleverly. You just have to notice there is a story, and that you are the one writing it.

Sometimes the story is the work. There is a pattern under the pattern, and you owe it the time. Sometimes, though, the feeling and the need are already enough. Try this on:

*I'm feeling insecure tonight, and I'm longing for some reassurance that you still find me attractive.*

That is a complete sentence. It does not require an excavation of every previous relationship, every adolescent humiliation, every glance caught in a mirror at fourteen. The feeling is named. The need is named. The request is in the longing. Save the archaeology for therapy, where someone is paid to hold the shovel.

This is where thoughtful people set their own trap. Over-excavation is its own avoidance — a way of staying in the head so the body doesn't have to be vulnerable in front of another person. *Let me figure out why I feel this* can be the longer, smarter version of *let me not say what I feel*. Analysis as costume. The need is still in there, waiting to be spoken plainly, while you go spelunking.

The quiet thing worth knowing is that the story changes once the feeling has run its course. What looked at 9 p.m. like proof of something turns, by morning, into mood. You have been wrong about this before. You don't always need to know why a feeling came; sometimes it is enough to let it pass and see what is left standing.

Most often what is left is the need. Speak from there.
