A few things, and then we are done for the night.

This is a practice, not a framework you master. You will not graduate. There is no certification that protects you from saying the wrong thing to your sister at Thanksgiving. The whole apparatus — feelings, needs, observations, requests — is a set of grooves you wear into yourself by repetition. Miki Kashtan calls this rewiring the nervous system, and she is not being metaphorical. The neurons that fire together do, eventually, wire together. The catch is that it takes years, not a weekend.

You will do it badly. You will do it badly tomorrow and probably again on Thursday. You will catch yourself mid-sentence, halfway through a perfectly executed observation, realizing you don't actually want to connect — you want to win, or be right, or be done. That is not a sign the practice failed. That is the practice. The noticing is the muscle. The not-noticing for forty years is what you came in with.

The goal is not to do NVC correctly. There is no NVC police. If there were, they would mostly be arresting people who use the structure as a power move, and that is not who this booklet is for. The goal is connection — a real one, the kind where two people walk away knowing slightly more than they did about what is actually going on between them. Sometimes connection looks like a paragraph of feelings and needs. Sometimes it looks like one of you saying *yeah, I know,* and the other one believing it.

If you want more, there is more. Rosenberg's book, which is more political than its reputation. Teachers who have spent decades inside this — Kashtan, Sofer, Meenadchi, others. Practice groups that meet in living rooms and over video calls and rarely advertise. This booklet is a door. The room is much larger and full of people who have been at it longer than you.

That's all. Try it on something small this week. See what moves.
