"I feel like you don't care." Try saying it out loud. Notice that the word *feel* is doing none of the work the sentence pretends it is. What follows it is not a feeling. It is a verdict — a small courtroom verdict about the other person's interior, dressed up in the soft cardigan of emotional language so it can pass through customs unsearched.

This is the trap. Most of what you call a feeling, on a hard day, is a thought about what someone else is doing to you. *I feel attacked. I feel ignored. I feel manipulated. I feel like you're not even trying.* These are not feelings. They are your hypothesis about another person's behavior, smuggled across the border under a feeling's passport. The body is not in the sentence. The other person is the entire sentence.

A real feeling lives in the body. Tight chest. Hot face. The cold thing low in the gut. Sad, scared, tired, lit up, restless, tender, furious, numb. These are yours. They are not arguments. They cannot be disputed because they are not claims; they are weather inside you, and weather does not require the other person's agreement to exist.

The pseudo-feeling — *attacked, ignored, dismissed, abandoned, used* — is a thought wearing a feeling's coat. It carries an accusation. It invites the other person to defend themselves rather than meet you. And here's the part that smarts: you probably reach for these constructions hardest in the moments when something real is going on inside you. The actual feeling is right there, often something more vulnerable than righteous, and the pseudo-feeling is the lid you put on it so you don't have to set it on the table.

This is a thing humans do. The author does it. You do it. The person who has been practicing this for thirty years still does it, just faster on the catch.

Underneath the feeling is the *need* — and here a piece of jargon arrives that the booklet will use without apology. In NVC, *needs* are not the things you want from a particular person. They are the universal stuff every human runs on: to matter, to be seen, to rest, to belong, to have some say over your own life, to be held, to be left alone, to make sense of things. The need is the why beneath the feeling. You feel hollow because something in you is hungry for connection. You feel taut because something in you is fighting for respect. The other person is, at most, the occasion. They are not the cause and they are not the cure.

This relocates the conversation. *I feel like you don't care* puts the whole drama inside the other person's character. *I'm hurt, and I'm longing to feel like I matter to you* — that puts it inside the room, where it can actually be answered.

A small aside, since it has been hovering: how certain are you, right now, that the thing you just called a feeling is a feeling? You have been wrong about this before. You have called *resentment* what was actually grief; you have called *anger* what was actually fear with a louder coat. Rosenberg goes further and calls anger itself a thinking event — the body's response to a story you are telling about wrongness. You don't have to swallow the whole claim to notice the part that stings: there is a story under the heat, and you wrote it.

The work, for now, is small. When the sentence starts with *I feel like* — pause. Something else is trying to speak. Find it in your body before you let your mouth name it.
