Someone you love tells you something hard. Their dog died, or their boss humiliated them, or the thing they have been afraid of for six months turned out to be true. Before they finish the sentence, your mouth is moving. You are explaining why it happened. You are giving them the larger frame. You are, with earnest tenderness, telling them what their experience means.

You have just done the most common thing there is, instead of being there.

This is the failure mode of listening, and it is almost never malice. Explanation feels like a gift. *The reason your boss did that is because he's threatened by competent women.* True, possibly. Useful, eventually. What the other person needed in that minute was not analysis. They needed someone to stay in the room with what just happened, before anyone reached for what it meant.

Then the move so unconscious it barely feels like one. They tell you their thing; within ten seconds you are telling them yours. *I know exactly what you mean, that happened to me last year…* This is the recentering trap, and it is hard to see in yourself because it almost always presents as connection. What is actually happening: the conversation has migrated to your interior, and theirs has been filed for later, if there is time. There usually isn't. The author does this. You do this.

A third move, faster: the leap to fixing. They have not finished the sentence and you are drafting the plan. Problem-solving is the love language of people who cannot bear another's distress — a way to get the painful feeling out of the room, because if they keep feeling it, you have to feel something near it.

Here is the part that changes things: you can listen with NVC even when the other person has never heard of NVC and is, at the moment, yelling. You do not need their cooperation. You can do this on your side, while they use none of the same tools. One of the more useful pieces of the whole practice.

What you are doing internally is simple to name and harder to do. You drop below the surface of what they are saying — the accusation, the story, the blame — and listen for the feeling and need underneath. They say *you never think about anyone but yourself* and what you are listening for is: something hurts, something matters to them and they are afraid it doesn't matter to you. Out loud it can be plain. *It sounds like that landed hard. Are you feeling unseen?* A real question with curiosity behind it.

The distinction worth holding: empathy is not a verbal technique, it is an orientation. Without the orientation you get the dead-eyed reflective-listening voice everyone has been subjected to — *what I'm hearing you say is that you feel…* — delivered with all the warmth of a parking meter. Words can be perfect and arrive empty.

Empathic listening is not silence, not agreement, not nodding while you wait for your turn, not problem-solving. It is the choice to keep your attention, a little longer than feels natural, on what the other person is feeling and needing — instead of the surface story, which, by the way, you have been wrong about before. You have felt the difference: the small unclenching when someone actually stayed with you. That is what you are offering. Not a fix. A place to land.

**The explanation trap.**

She tells you, voice flat the way voices go when something has been carried too long, that her father is in the hospital again. You hear yourself say: *With his history it was kind of inevitable.*

What lands instead: *God. You've been carrying this a long time. Are you scared?*

**The recentering trap.**

He says the layoff blindsided him. You say: *That happened to me in 2019, the best thing I ever did was—*

What lands instead: *That sounds disorienting. Do you want to talk it through, or just have somebody know?* And then — this is the part — you stop talking.

**The advice trap.**

They tell you their kid is struggling at school. Before they take a breath you are running through tutors, schedules, screen-time, the book your friend swore by. Their face does that small closing thing.

What lands instead: *That sounds exhausting. How are you doing inside it?* The plan can come later, and will come better if it does.

**Asymmetric use.**

He is not speaking NVC. He is speaking the language of *you always* and *you never*, with some volume. Internally, you are doing two things at once: keeping your own nervous system from going to war, and listening for what is underneath the heat — not to excuse it, to find what it is made of.

Out loud, eventually: *It sounds like you've been feeling like this doesn't matter to me. Is that close?* You may be wrong. He will tell you. The point is not the perfect guess; it is that you stayed pointed at him instead of your own defense.

You will do this badly. So will everyone trying it with you. Keep going.
