*We are working from stories, not facts. You have been wrong about this before. The story changes once the feeling has run its course.* Different ways of saying one thing.

The thing they add up to: NVC is not, structurally, a communication technique. It is a piece of working magic. We can say this now because the booklet has spent several thousand words showing it to you in slow motion.

By *magic* we do not mean the woo. We mean the older, operative sense — what Alan Moore is doing when he points out that *to cast a spell is simply to spell, to manipulate words, to change people's consciousness*, and *a grimoire is just a grammar.* What Robert Anton Wilson is doing when he calls the verb *to be* a *deity mode of speech.* Some sentences do not describe the world; they enact something in it. They marry. They convict. They bless. They curse. *I now pronounce you* is not a description of the event. It is the event.

Consider the basic move of NVC, then.

*You are selfish* renders Elizabeth permanently. It uses the deity mode of speech: it places a permanent quality onto a person, in a moment, by an authority — yours — and the only response available is contestation. Either Elizabeth accepts your verdict and reorganizes herself around your judgment, or she defends, and you escalate, and the verdict gets sharper with each exchange.

*You didn't text me back for two days, and I am hurt, and I am longing for partnership* does not render Elizabeth at all. It reports an event that happened in a body, in a moment, for a reason. There is no permanence claim. There is no verdict. The other person can meet the longing or not, but they cannot be made permanent by the sentence. They are not on trial. They are being met.

The same grammar holds when you are the one who has been harmed. *He is an abuser* is the is-of-identity aimed in a direction that feels righteous — it might even feel accurate — but it works by the same mechanism. It renders a person permanent by their worst behavior. Harm describes behavior. Behavior can be named, accounted for, sometimes changed. The person is not the act, even when the act has been serious and the pattern long.

Knowing that harm describes behavior rather than freezing a person into a permanent role is precision, not minimization. One person's behavior can be far more harmful. One person can go far deeper into denial, reversal, and coercion. The grammar still matters: we name the pattern without rendering anyone ontologically evil. This leaves room for real accountability instead of permanent verdicts.

Including, sometimes, yourself — the version of you who did something you cannot forgive, who you have been rendering permanent ever since.

That is the working magic. It is structurally the same move D. David Bourland Jr. coded as E-Prime — English without *to be* — and the same move Alfred Korzybski had been pointing at since 1933 with his critique of the *is of identity.* Marshall Rosenberg appears to have arrived at it without going through general semantics; the resemblance is striking enough that someone, eventually, should write the comparative essay. The booklet is doing it informally now.

The reason this matters is that the language is not just describing the speaker's interior. It is *changing* it. Saying *you are selfish* installs a particular state in your nervous system: prosecutorial, certain, slightly armored, ready to fight. Saying *I am hurt and I am longing for partnership* installs a different state: vulnerable, located in the body, no longer at war. The grammar makes the speaker into a particular kind of person at the moment of speaking. So does the silence afterward. Speech is, in this sense, performative all the way down. It does not narrate the self. It builds the self, every time you open your mouth.

If that sounds like a strong claim, it is. It is also the claim Marshall Rosenberg himself was making, and he was making it more sharply than his reputation would suggest.

From the second chapter of *Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life:*

> *Life-alienating communication both stems from and supports hierarchical or domination societies, where large populations are controlled by a small number of individuals to those individuals' own benefit... The language of wrongness, should, and have to is perfectly suited for this purpose: the more people are trained to think in terms of moralistic judgments that imply wrongness and badness, the more they are being trained to look outside themselves — to outside authorities — for the definition of what constitutes right, wrong, good, and bad. When we are in contact with our feelings and needs, we humans no longer make good slaves and underlings.*

That is not a self-help sentence. That is structurally an anarchist sentence. It identifies the language of moral judgment as the linguistic substrate of obedience. It identifies the practice of locating one's own feelings and needs as a refusal of that obedience.

The booklet is not going to argue this at length, but it is going to name it, because the field tends to pretend Rosenberg didn't write it.

NVC's deepest commitment, when read seriously, is to a kind of sovereignty — yours, and everyone else's. *Connection over control* is the slogan. The substance underneath the slogan is that nobody, including you, has the authority to render anyone else permanent. Stories about people are stories. The person is still the person. They are allowed to surprise you. They are allowed, even, to surprise themselves. The same goes for you. The story you have been telling yourself about yourself for the past twenty years is also a story.

This is why NVC sits next to the anarchist mediation lineages, and why those lineages have been doing some of the most interesting NVC work in the world. Restorative-justice circle practices developed in Brazilian favelas. Consensus-based facilitation work prefigurative organizers would recognize on sight. Decolonizing-NVC writers pointing out that the practice's standard packaging often re-installs the dominator culture it was meant to refuse — and that it has to be retooled to do its actual work in bodies it wasn't originally designed for. None of this is fringe; it is the part of NVC the workshop pamphlets tend to soften. (Names and pointers in the back-matter.)

All of these approaches share the same refusal of a single, fixed, judging self.

It is also why the IFS people and the somatic trauma people have all, independently, found their way into NVC's adjacent territory. Because the underlying claim of the practice — that the self is not a unitary thing pronouncing on the world from a fixed throne, but a moving collection of feelings, needs, parts, and bodies, all of which deserve precise language — is a claim Internal Family Systems makes from one direction, that polyvagal-informed somatic work makes from another, that Buddhist parts-work makes from a third. NVC translates between them with a small, portable grammar.

That is, finally, what we mean by saying NVC is a hinge. It is not a tradition unto itself. It is not the room. It is a door between rooms — between IFS and codependence recovery, between somatic trauma work and anarchist mediation, between Korzybski's general semantics and the dharma traditions that have been pointing at the same thing for two and a half thousand years. Most of the practice's actual value, lived inside, is in this translator role. The booklet's view, for what it is worth, is that the people who have hardened NVC into a primary identity have mostly missed what makes the practice worth doing. The people who hold it lightly, and use it as a tool inside a larger life, tend to do better with it.

**A note on practice and time.**

The booklet has been telling you, in different ways, that NVC at first sounds stilted. *I'm feeling X and I'm needing Y.* Yes. Yes it does. We have established this.

The thing the booklet has not quite said yet, and should say before the conclusion, is what happens after you have been doing this for years.

The scaffolding falls away. People who have been doing this for a long time do not, in conversation, sound like they are using NVC. They sound like themselves. They sound, if anything, slightly more like themselves than most people sound, because the *to-be* verbs and the moralistic judgments and the demands disguised as requests have been quietly evicted from their sentences over time. They do not require their feelings to be agreed with. They do not require their stories to be the official record. They do not press their version of you onto you. They are, in a small and durable way, more sovereign in conversation than most people are. And so are the people they are talking to, because the form of speech offers that sovereignty as a side-effect, whether or not the other person ever picks up the practice themselves.

What the practice does, given enough time, is not what the marketing claims. It does not make you nicer. It makes you *clearer* — clearer to yourself first, then to whoever is in the room with you. It does not, despite some of the workshop language, make you a calmer person. It makes you a more accurately located person. The fury, when fury is what is in the room, is still in the sentence. It is just no longer pretending to be a description of somebody else.

In the way that contact improv is, for the people who have practiced it long enough, a spiritual relationship practice — not a religion, not a worldview, not a metaphysics, just a discipline of paying attention to weight and contact and movement that opens, over years, into something larger than the technique — NVC, practiced long enough, becomes something larger than the four-step. It is a practice of speaking and listening from the present moment, in the body, with the other person's interior treated as real and legitimate, and one's own certainty held more lightly than your nervous system would prefer.

That is the magic. That is also the dharma, if you want to call it that. There isn't anything more to it than that. There also doesn't need to be.
