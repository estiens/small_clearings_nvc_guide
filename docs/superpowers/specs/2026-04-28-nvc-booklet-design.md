# NVC Booklet — Writing Brief
*2026-04-28*

## Purpose

A ~20-page introduction to Nonviolent Communication for friends who would roll their eyes at the Rosenberg book — skeptics, cynics, and curious newcomers alike. A sneak attack on the large population of humans who mistake stories for facts, thoughts for feelings, and feelings for requests without knowing they do it.

Not self-help. Not therapy. Something with more grit than that.

---

## Audience

Primary: skeptics/cynics burned by therapy-speak + curious newcomers who haven't encountered NVC.
Secondary (covert): people who don't know they need this — the ones who don't differentiate facts from stories, who mistake evaluations for feelings, who confuse requests with emotional states.

The booklet doesn't announce itself as being for them. It just is.

---

## Entry Point

Direct address. The first page names what NVC is **not** — not a script, not therapy, not a technique for being nicer, not a way to win arguments more politely. The failure mode that opens the book: the conversation where everyone said the right words and nothing connected.

---

## Structure (Problem-First)

Each section opens with a failure mode — the actual mess — and reverse-engineers why NVC addresses it. Philosophy surfaces as the accumulated weight of every reframe, made explicit only in the final section.

### 1. Intro — What This Is Not
- Direct address, irreverent, disarming
- Opens with the failure mode: the conversation where everything said was technically fine and nothing landed
- Names what NVC isn't before explaining what it is
- Seeds the epistemological throughline quietly: we are working from stories, not facts

### 2. Feelings + Needs
- Opens with the "I feel like you don't care" problem — a thought dressed as a feeling
- Covers: feeling vs. evaluation, pseudo-feelings, universal needs as the underlying layer
- Throughline: how certain are you that what you just called a feeling is a feeling?

### 3. Speaking with NVC + Examples
- Opens with the complaint-as-NVC trap (using the structure to just voice grievances, forgetting empathy)
- OFNR introduced without making it feel like a formula
- The "what I'm longing for" positive reframe lives here
- Traps named: formulaic use, fighting over who's doing NVC correctly, using structure as a weapon
- Examples page: 4-6 entries, mix of subtle and aggressive failures, literary texture

### 4. Self-NVC (Bridge Section)
- The pause: "this is a feeling — what story am I telling myself about it?"
- When to excavate the story and when to stay with the feeling and the need
- Key example: "I'm feeling insecure tonight and I'm longing for reassurance that you find me attractive" — no excavation required, feeling + need is enough
- Save the full story archaeology for your own therapy

### 5. Listening with NVC + Examples
- Opens with the explanation-instead-of-empathy trap (offering your account instead of their feeling)
- The recentering trap: someone who keeps pulling the conversation back to themselves
- Asymmetric use: you can listen with NVC even when the other person isn't speaking it
- Examples page: 4-6 entries, includes reframes of what empathy actually sounds like vs. what we usually do

### 6. Philosophy — Why This Is Actually Radical
- Epistemological humility made explicit: how many times has your story of events changed in retrospect? How many times have you been certain about what someone meant and been wrong?
- Feelings run their course and interpretation changes — this is not a failure, it's how it works
- Connection over control. Empathy over assumptions and "correctness."
- Radical equality and sovereignty: NVC demands you treat the other person's inner life as real and legitimate, not as a problem to be managed
- Anarchist throughline (named but not argued — one or two sentences): non-violence as a practice in refusing coercion, including the coercive certainty of our own stories. The connection is noted, not explained at length.

### 7. Conclusion — It's a Practice
- Not a theory. Not a framework you master. Brain rewiring through repetition.
- You will do it badly. So will everyone you practice with.
- The goal is not NVC-compliance. The goal is connection.
- End on the realistic and slightly tender: this stuff is hard, it matters, keep going

## Voice & Tone

**Register:** Poetic, gritty, dry. A retired couples therapist and longtime mediator who used to be a punk and had serious anger management issues. The authority is earned through failure, not credential.

**The voice is a tonal register, not a character.** No autobiography. No first-person anecdotes. The voice shows up as attitude and precision, not memoir.

**What this means in practice:**
- Short sentences when something matters. Longer, winding ones when we're inside a feeling or a failure mode
- Profanity earns its place — used when the clean version would be dishonest
- Traps named with precision and a little warmth — "this is a thing humans do, including the author, including you"
- No inspirational register. Nothing that sounds like a TED talk or a wellness blog
- The epistemological throughline surfaces as asides: "which, by the way, you have been wrong about before"
- Dark humor that's true first, funny second
- Trauma-informed: the book knows that some of these conversations happen under conditions of real pain and history

---

## Examples Approach

Each example section (after Speaking and Listening): 4-6 entries, no example longer than half a page, most much shorter.

**Mix includes:**
- Subtle failures (evaluation dressed as feeling: "I feel like this is a pattern with you")
- Aggressive failures ("you never fucking care about anything I do around here") — rephrased, but the real need underneath honored
- Traps in action (empathy that's actually just your own story reflected back)
- A longing reframe that models the positive without being saccharine

**Texture:** Literary, not clinical. The examples lean into common failure modes in ways that feel real, not constructed for demonstration purposes.

---

## Traps to Name (distributed throughout)

- Doing NVC *for* your partner — as a power move, passive-aggressively
- Fighting over who is or isn't using NVC correctly
- Using it too formulaically (the robot problem)
- Using the structure to voice complaints without offering empathy
- Getting stuck in the negative; forgetting the "what I'm longing for" move
- Not pausing to find the feeling before excavating the story
- Excavating the story when the feeling + need is already there and sufficient
- Listening trap: offering explanations instead of empathy
- Listening trap: the person who keeps recentering themselves
- Forgetting that asymmetric use is valid — you don't need the other person to be doing NVC for you to listen with it

---

## Philosophy Throughline (not heavy-handed)

The argument running underneath everything: we are not working from facts, we are working from stories. Stories change. Feelings run their course and interpretation shifts. We have been wrong, repeatedly, about what other people meant. NVC is partly a communication practice and partly a discipline of holding our own certainty more lightly.

This connects to: radical equality (the other person's inner life is as real and legitimate as yours), sovereignty (connection over control), non-violence as refusing coercion including the coercive certainty of our own narratives.

The philosophy section makes this explicit. The rest of the booklet enacts it.

---

## Success Definition

The reader finishes knowing: (1) there is a real thing here, with depth, a history, communities of practice, and many years of development — not just a technique; and (2) they have been doing something without knowing it, and it is survivable and changeable. The booklet is an on-ramp, not a destination. The conclusion points outward — communities, the Rosenberg book for those who want to go deeper — without being a bibliography.

---

## NVC Culture as Foil

The traps named in this booklet are not specific to NVC. Any tool can be weaponized; any practice can harden into formula or be used as a power move. One sentence to that effect, early, and then we move on. The booklet does not position itself against NVC culture; it positions itself against the failure modes that belong to all of us.

---

## Philosophy Rhythm

The epistemological argument is not a pivot at section 6 — it surfaces as quiet asides throughout every section. By the time the philosophy section arrives, the reader has already encountered the pattern (we work from stories, not facts; we have been wrong before; feelings run their course and interpretation changes) without it being named. Section 6 names what has been there all along. It should feel like recognition, not introduction.

---

## Reader Recognition Space

The examples are designed to create "oh, that's me" moments — not "here is a person doing the wrong thing." The specificity of the failure modes does this work. The booklet stays observational in voice; the recognition happens in the reader, not on the page.

---

## What This Is Not

- Not the Rosenberg book (no extended theory, no case studies, no academic apparatus)
- Not an NVC certification manual
- Not therapy
- Not inspirational
- Not for people who already think they're good communicators (though it's especially for them)

## Target Length & Density

~4,000-4,500 words total. Section targets:
- Intro: ~350 words — punchy, no room to wander
- Feelings + Needs: ~550 words
- Speaking + Examples: ~750 words
- Self-NVC Bridge: ~300 words — a hinge, not a chapter
- Listening + Examples: ~750 words
- Philosophy: ~550 words
- Conclusion: ~350 words

Nothing padded. Adjustable as drafting reveals what needs more or less room.
