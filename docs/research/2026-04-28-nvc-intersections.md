# NVC Intersections: Research Pass

*A loaded-context document for the booklet. Five intersections, then a synthesis.
Citations are inline; this is not an annotated bibliography.*

---

## 1. NVC + IFS (Internal Family Systems)

This intersection is unexpectedly well-developed. Several practitioners have done the explicit bridging work, and there's now a small ecosystem of NVC+IFS trainings, podcasts, and writing.

The most useful framing across the literature is that NVC and IFS share a core wager — that the self is plural, not a unified "mono-mind," and that compassionate inner relationship is the prerequisite for compassionate outer relationship. The site Compassionate Self ("When NVC Meets IFS: A Powerful Combination for Self-Awareness and Empathy") puts it bluntly: IFS sharpens NVC by helping practitioners get specific about *which part* is feeling/needing what; NVC sharpens IFS by giving the parts a precise grammar to speak in. Without IFS, NVC can flatten an internal multiplicity into a single first-person voice that doesn't actually exist. Without NVC, IFS parts work can stay interior — never landing as repair in actual relationships.

Sarah Peyton is the booklet's obvious anchor here. Her *Your Resonant Self* (2017, with foreword by Bonnie Badenoch) and the workbook that followed weave Default Mode Network neuroscience, NVC's feelings/needs grammar, and an explicit parts-work move: she names a structure called the Resonating Self-Witness (RSW), a part you cultivate that resonates with — "guesses for" — other parts that need to be heard. Peyton frames this as deliberately rewiring the inner critic by giving the brain warm, precise, non-judgmental language. RSW is functionally Self-energy in IFS terms, but voiced in NVC syntax. She also runs constellations work, which gives her a third leg most NVC trainers don't have.

Beyond Peyton: the **Lead Your Life** foundation course (offered through CNVC and NVC-UK) is a two-day program explicitly billed as "Integration of Internal Family Systems with NVC," advertising itself as a path to a trauma-informed NVC practice. The Authentic Communication Group ("Integrating IFS and NVC for Deeper Connection") and the Compassionate Self collective both market integrated trainings. Spencer Thompson appeared on *The One Inside* IFS podcast specifically on "IFS and Tuning Into Needs with Non Violent Communication." The Parts & Self project ("Two Hearts, One Beat") frames the two as a single emerging paradigm of plural mind.

The most distinctive bridge already built: NVC's four-step (observation, feeling, need, request) gets repurposed as the *self-talk script you use to greet a part*. Instead of "What am I feeling?" you ask "Which part of me is here, what is it observing, what does it feel, what does it need, what does it want from me?" That re-grammars NVC away from confessional first-person and toward a form of internal mediation.

**Where it's still under-explored:** the politics. None of the public NVC+IFS material seems to ask whether IFS's "Self" smuggles in a unitary-soul metaphysics that NVC's needs-talk can also drift toward. There's also little serious writing on when the two frames *disagree* — e.g., IFS would tend to honor a protector's strategy as protective; NVC would press toward the unmet need underneath. The booklet has room to play there.

## 2. NVC + Magick

This is the thinnest intersection in terms of explicit existing literature, and that's a real finding for the booklet — *no one seems to have written explicitly and at length about NVC as magickal practice.* What exists is adjacent material that the booklet can stitch together itself.

The closest existing bridge is general semantics. Marshall Rosenberg's distinction between observation and evaluation is structurally identical to Alfred Korzybski's critique of the "is of identity" in *Science and Sanity* (1933) — the move that "Elizabeth is a fool" treats a momentary act as an immutable essence. Korzybski's student D. David Bourland, Jr. coined E-Prime: English without any form of "to be." Robert Anton Wilson then made E-Prime a chaos-magick-adjacent technology, framing the verb "to be" as a "deity mode of speech" that allows even an idiot to "transform their opinions magically into god-like pronouncements on the nature of things" (Kellogg & Bourland, paraphrased by RAW). Wilson's claim — drop "is," add "maybe," and people will act dramatically more sane — is functionally the same move Rosenberg makes when he insists "you are lazy" be reformulated as "when I see the dishes still in the sink at 9 pm, I feel frustrated because I'm needing partnership." NVC is, structurally, applied E-Prime with feelings/needs vocabulary bolted on. Whether Rosenberg knew the general-semantics lineage isn't clearly documented in current sources (could not verify Rosenberg explicitly citing Korzybski).

Alan Moore is the other natural ally. Moore's well-known formulation — "to cast a spell is simply 'to spell,' to manipulate words and change people's consciousness," and "a grimoire is just a grammar" (from *On Magic* and *The Moon and Serpent Bumper Book of Magic*) — is the booklet's gift. NVC is, in Moore's frame, a grimoire: a grammar designed to alter the consciousness of speaker and hearer simultaneously. Moore's 1993 declaration that art and magic are the same — both "the science of manipulating symbols, words, or images, to achieve changes in consciousness" — is an almost too-perfect parallel to Rosenberg's claim that NVC is a "language of life" that "manifests a certain spirituality."

Speech-act theory (J.L. Austin, *How to Do Things with Words*, 1962) provides the secular scaffolding for the same intuition: that some utterances *do* things rather than describing them — they marry, they convict, they bless. NVC's "I feel X because I'm needing Y, would you be willing to Z?" is a performative in Austin's sense — it doesn't describe a state, it *enacts* a request and re-positions the speaker from blame to vulnerability in real time.

Rosenberg himself flirted with magick-adjacent vocabulary. He talked about NVC as "an attempt to manifest a certain spirituality" and used the phrase "Beloved Divine Energy" — language critics have flagged as cult-shaped (see Collectively Free's "Nonviolent Communication is for the Privileged"; UUA's LeaderLab piece on NVC and dominant culture). The booklet can cheerfully point out that the critics are correct about the structure and wrong about the verdict: yes, NVC is a magical practice; no, that doesn't make it a fraud.

**Where it's under-explored:** essentially everywhere. There is no Alan-Moore-meets-Marshall-Rosenberg essay in the searchable web. No chaos magick zine has claimed NVC as a working. The booklet is doing fresh work here.

## 3. NVC + Anarchism

Richer than expected, though scattered. The political content is *in* Rosenberg — "life-alienating communication both stems from and supports hierarchical or domination societies… the more people are trained to think in terms of moralistic judgments that imply wrongness and badness, the more they are being trained to look outside themselves to outside authorities for the definition of what constitutes right, wrong, good, and bad" (*Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life*, ch. 2). That sentence is structurally an anarchist sentence. It identifies moral-judgment language as the linguistic substrate of obedience.

Several practitioners run with this. Miki Kashtan (*The Highest Common Denominator*, *Reweaving Our Human Fabric*; The Fearless Heart) frames patriarchy as "the principle of separation, scarcity, and control" and treats NVC as one tool inside a larger nonviolent-global-liberation project. She is explicit that NVC at its honest scale is a project of dismantling domination, not a politeness training. Her Nonviolent Global Liberation (NGL) community runs explicitly along consensus-and-collaboration lines — close in form to anarchist prefigurative organizing.

Roxy Manning (*The Antiracist Heart*, 2023; *How to Have Antiracist Conversations*) and Edmundo Norte are the leading voices in decolonizing NVC. Manning's critique is sharp: NVC's packaging assumes a white middle-class individualism, and its delivery has often re-inscribed the very dominator culture it claims to critique. Norte explicitly frames decolonization as a project of moving from the "domination culture" toward Indigenous relational ways. Meenadchi's small zine-format book *Decolonizing Non-Violent Communication* (2019) is a key text for this thread.

The mediation lineage is the clearest formal bridge. Dominic Barter's **Restorative Circles**, developed in Rio de Janeiro favelas and adopted by Brazil's Ministry of Justice from 2005, are structurally NVC-rooted and structurally non-state — they relocate accountability to the local community using "author of an act," "recipient," and "community" instead of "offender" and "victim." This is the one place where NVC has produced something a contemporary abolitionist can actually use without modification.

CrimethInc.'s *Accounting for Ourselves* (2013) on accountability processes in anarchist scenes explicitly names "nonviolent communication" as one of the skill-sets these processes draw on — though the essay is also clear-eyed about how community accountability work has often failed when it has leaned too hard on NVC-style emotional protocols without addressing power asymmetry. That tension is itself useful: NVC by itself is not abolitionist practice, but it sits inside abolitionist tooling.

The skeptical anarchist take exists too: Peter Gelderloos's *The Failure of Nonviolence* (Anarchist Library) doesn't engage NVC specifically but argues that "nonviolence" as ideology serves the state. The booklet should know this critique exists — NVC as a method is not the same as nonviolence as a movement strategy, and conflating them is a common error.

**Where it's under-explored:** there is no widely known "anarchist NVC" book, no Crimethinc-NVC-zine. The gap between the radical-political content of Rosenberg's claims and the typically liberal-self-help aesthetic of NVC trainings is the single largest unaddressed embarrassment in the field. The booklet has a clean line here.

## 4. NVC + Codependence

Stronger than expected. There is an explicit, named bridge: **NVC Codependency Recovery**, a PuddleDancer Press resource that maps NVC tools directly onto the patterns of recovery articulated by Codependents Anonymous (CoDA). The framing in that material: codependency is "a compulsive or habitual style of relating with others… outward focused, characterized by display of learned roles (caregiver, hero, people-pleaser) and lack of awareness of one's own feelings and needs in the moment." That sentence is almost a definition of *what NVC is designed to interrupt.* If codependence is the inability to know your own feelings/needs in real time, NVC is — at minimum — the literacy class.

The classic recovery canon (Pia Mellody's *Facing Codependence*, 1989; Melody Beattie's *Codependent No More*, 1986) doesn't cite NVC, and NVC's official literature rarely cites them — but the diagnostic overlap is substantial. Mellody's "five primary adult symptoms" (difficulty experiencing appropriate levels of self-esteem, setting functional boundaries, owning one's reality, addressing wants and needs, and experiencing/expressing reality moderately) overlap so precisely with NVC's diagnostic frame (you confuse observations with evaluations, you don't know your feelings, you don't know your needs, you ask in demands) that they read like translations of each other. CoDA's own "Patterns of Recovery" and "Communication and Recovery" pamphlets explicitly include "asking directly for what we want" as a recovery pattern — which is just the request step of NVC under another name.

The most useful framing the booklet can steal from this lineage: people-pleasing isn't a personality flaw, it's a *learned survival strategy* that worked. NVC, read through codependence recovery, isn't asking you to become more authentic; it's asking your nervous system to risk that asking directly might not get you killed this time. The recovery tradition supplies the developmental trauma backdrop NVC pedagogy often skips. This is also where NVC's "demand vs. request" distinction lands hardest — the codependent has spent a lifetime issuing demands disguised as requests because direct asking was too dangerous, and reading hints they couldn't safely make explicit.

Inner-critic work is the natural overlap. Sarah Peyton's RSW (see Section 1) and her work on the Default Mode Network as the seat of self-criticism is fully consonant with Mellody's "carried feelings" and inner-child work — both name an internalized voice of childhood adaptation that needs warmth, not argument.

The IFS+codependence literature is also dense (e.g., IFS-EMDR Therapy's "People-Pleasing & Codependency: How Childhood Patterns Heal") and provides a third triangulation point: protector parts that learned to scan for everyone else's needs first.

**Where it's under-explored:** the *failure mode* direction. Almost everything written treats NVC as a *recovery aid* for codependents. Almost nothing examines how NVC trainings can become a new arena for codependent patterning — over-empathizing, suppressing requests because you don't want to "create a demand," confusing self-abandonment with compassion. The booklet has a real opening to name NVC as a place where codependence often *re-installs itself in spiritualized form.*

## 5. NVC + Trauma / Somatics

Deepest and most active intersection. This is where most of the live thinking in NVC right now seems to be happening.

**Sarah Peyton** is again the central node — *Your Resonant Self* (2017) and *The Resonant Self Workbook* (2021) are the bridge texts. Peyton synthesizes interpersonal neurobiology (Daniel Siegel, Bonnie Badenoch), Default Mode Network research, and NVC's grammar into a single practice. Her central technical claim: trauma fragments the relational brain, and *resonant language* — precise, warm, accurate guesses about what someone is feeling and needing — physically rewires it. Empathy, in this frame, is not a virtue but a neurological intervention.

**Kathleen Macferran** (Strength of Connection; *Choice: A Field Guide for Navigating the Polarization of Our World*, with Jared Finkelstein) is one of the leading trauma-informed NVC trainers. Her core framing — repeated almost verbatim in the PuddleDancer "NVC Trauma" resource — is that "you cannot move the conversation faster than the ability of everyone's nervous system to digest, assimilate, or integrate the conversation." This is the booklet's somatic license to slow everything down. Macferran's work explicitly bridges Polyvagal Theory (Stephen Porges) and NVC: ventral vagal = the only state in which the four-step actually works; sympathetic and dorsal states need somatic regulation *first*, words second. NVC without nervous-system literacy becomes a coercion of the prefrontal cortex over a body that hasn't yet decided it's safe.

**Resmaa Menakem** (*My Grandmother's Hands*, 2017) doesn't write about NVC directly, but his framework is the missing somatic-and-racialized layer. Menakem trained with Peter Levine, Bessel van der Kolk, and David Schnarch; he insists racialized trauma lives in the body and must be discharged somatically before any cognitive work — including NVC — can land. The implication for NVC: a white-comfortable feelings-and-needs script applied to a body holding centuries of inherited terror can re-traumatize. Menakem's Cultural Somatics work pairs naturally with Roxy Manning's antiracist NVC for any practitioner trying to take the body seriously.

**Peter Levine's** Somatic Experiencing, **Pat Ogden's** Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, **Deb Dana's** Polyvagal Theory in Therapy (and *Polyvagal Exercises for Safety and Connection*), and **Bessel van der Kolk's** *The Body Keeps the Score* are all in adjacent orbit. None of them cite NVC explicitly in the searchable literature, but the trauma-informed NVC trainings (Macferran, Peyton, the Lead Your Life programs) draw directly from this canon. Dana's framing of co-regulation — that one ventral-vagal nervous system can offer another nervous system "cues of safety" through facial tone, vocal prosody, and rhythm — is functionally what an NVC empathy listener is doing when they slow down and reflect feelings. Empathy is co-regulation with extra steps.

The most distinctive bridge already in print: Peyton's claim that the speaker's *guess for* the other person — "Are you feeling X because you're needing Y?" — works precisely because the brain is built for resonance, and being warmly and accurately misread is more healing than being correctly understood without warmth. This collapses the typical NVC-purist anxiety about "guessing right."

**Where it's under-explored:** the dorsal-vagal / freeze direction. Most NVC pedagogy assumes the speaker can locate a feeling and a need. Trauma-informed NVC has good material on slowing down a sympathetic-activated speaker, but less on what to do when someone is functionally numb — when "I don't know what I feel" is the honest answer. The booklet can sit in that space.

## Synthesis

Across all five intersections, three threads keep showing up.

**First, NVC works as a translator.** It's a small, portable grammar that converts between systems that already exist — IFS parts to needs language, codependent self-abandonment to direct request, somatic activation to articulable feeling, anarchist anti-authority politics to interpersonal practice, the magickal claim that words alter reality to a five-line script you can use at dinner. The booklet's voice can lean into this: NVC is less an original tradition than a hinge that lets adjacent traditions speak to each other.

**Second, NVC is most honest when it admits it's a practice, not a politeness.** The trauma, IFS, codependence, and decolonizing voices all converge on the same point: the four-step is meaningless without a regulated nervous system, an interior multiplicity, a developmental-trauma-aware view of why people-pleasing feels like survival, and a power analysis of who gets penalized for using it. The booklet's punk-dharma move is to refuse the self-help-seminar register and stand inside the practice with all that adjacent freight loaded.

**Third, the magick angle is genuinely fresh.** No one in the visible NVC literature has explicitly named NVC as a magickal technology — a grimoire, a working, a performative incantation that does things to rooms. RAW, Alan Moore, Korzybski, and J.L. Austin are all in the same conceptual neighborhood as Rosenberg without anyone having walked between the houses. That's the booklet's clearest contribution: naming what NVC actually does in the room, in language that doesn't apologize for being weird about it.
